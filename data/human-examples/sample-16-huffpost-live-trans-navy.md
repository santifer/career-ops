The segment I produced at HuffPost Live in 2012 or 2013 didn't feel historic at the time. It felt like a Tuesday. A source booking came together, the rundown slot opened, and I put together a panel on transgender military service that included an anonymous active-duty Navy service member — no face, no surname, call sign "Olivia" — who was at active risk of discharge for participating. That broadcast aired four years before the Pentagon reversed its policy in June 2016. When the Trump administration's trans military ban surfaced in 2017, AJ+ pulled the segment as archival evidence. The story had aged into primary source documentation.
What the segment required — and what I want to name precisely because it's the part that doesn't make it onto a résumé — was a particular kind of editorial judgment that only operates under pressure from multiple directions simultaneously. HuffPost Live was already running under an "attack the attacker" legal posture from its Scientology coverage. Every segment decision, chyron, and source booking was subject to multi-million-dollar legal scrutiny. I wasn't navigating that framework abstractly. I was inside it, at the segment level, making real-time calls about what we could put on air, how we named people, and what disclosures the broadcast required. Booking an anonymous active-duty service member into that environment — someone facing genuine discharge risk — meant the legal exposure wasn't hypothetical. The sourcing decision itself carried consequences that could land on the organization, the guest, and the story.
The panel I assembled around "Olivia" was built to hold the segment at that level of sensitivity. Bryn Tanhill was a trans Navy pilot; Monica Helms had served on a Navy submarine and founded the Transgender American Veterans Association; David McKean came from the Servicemembers Legal Defense Network; Sue Fulton represented OutServe. That wasn't a diversity-of-viewpoint booking strategy — it was a sourcing architecture. Every seat at the table had institutional standing, documented expertise, and the kind of on-record credibility that insulates a litigation-sensitive broadcast from the weakest-link attack. When you're operating under that legal posture, the panel itself is part of the editorial defense. I understood that, and I built accordingly.
The segment ran without retraction or public blowback. Four years later, the Pentagon's June 2016 policy reversal validated what the panel had argued on-air before the policy debate had fully surfaced in mainstream coverage. A year after that, when the political environment reversed again, AJ+ — a separate organization I'd joined after leaving HuffPost Live — resurfaced the segment as archival documentation during the 2017 trans military ban debate. That's a specific kind of result: a segment that held up not just editorially but evidentiary, under conditions its original producers couldn't have anticipated.
What I take from that segment now — and why I keep returning to it when the job description says "partner with senior media leaders" — is that the editorial judgment required to produce it wasn't separable from the institutional judgment. You can't book an anonymous active-duty service member into a litigation-sensitive broadcast environment without understanding how the legal posture constrains the segment design, how the sourcing architecture functions as institutional protection, and how the story will age against the policy curve you've identified. That's not segment-producer thinking. That's the same systems-under-pressure discipline I brought to autonomous agent deployment at Google, where the stakes were different but the logic was identical: build something that holds up when no one is watching it, under conditions you can't fully predict at the time you ship it. The HuffPost Live segment ran unretracted through four years of political volatility. My comms triage agent ran unattended through a multi-month absence without a single human intervention. The instinct that produced both of those outcomes is the same instinct — and it's exactly what senior media partnership work requires when the editorial decisions carry real institutional weight.
