The collaboration pattern I built at Google's Office of Cross-Google Engineering wasn't something I negotiated into my job description. It emerged from a structural reality: I was the only non-engineer embedded inside a horizontal engineering organization whose entire mandate was driving technical change across all of Google — and my work product had to survive contact with roughly a thousand of the sharpest technical critics in the company.
xGE serves Google's most senior individual contributor population — Principal, Distinguished, and Google Fellow engineers, the top 0.5% of a 180,000-person organization. These are people who will flag imprecision in a communication the way a compiler flags a type error. They don't extend charity to vague language, and they don't mistake polish for substance. If what I shipped wasn't technically accurate, structurally sound, and honest about tradeoffs, it came back marked up. The feedback loop was immediate and had no soft edges.
What that environment forced, over time, was a working methodology I didn't have a name for until I started describing it in interviews: I learned to build draft cycles that treated Distinguished Engineers as co-authors, not approvers. The distinction matters. An approver reviews something finished and says yes or no. A co-author gets pulled in early, when the architecture of the thing is still malleable — when their input can actually change what gets built. In practice, this meant sitting with engineers before I had a draft, asking the structural question first: what does this communication need to accomplish technically, and where does the honest complexity live? Then I'd come back with a working draft that named the tradeoffs explicitly, flagged the parts I'd simplified, and invited correction. Engineers responded to that differently than to a polished brief that assumed its own accuracy. It lowered the temperature of the review cycle because it was honest about the limits of my own domain knowledge from the jump.
The three production LLM agents I shipped inside xGE were built through exactly this dynamic. The autonomous communications triage system — a three-prompt architecture running triage, revision, and escalation with conditional knowledge-base loading — went through multiple structural iterations with senior engineers before it reached production. Not because the prompts were wrong, but because the classification logic for what counted as High-touch versus Low-touch had to be calibrated against how these engineers actually thought about the relative urgency of different inbound traffic types. I couldn't reverse-engineer that from first principles. I had to ask, watch how they described their own priorities, and rebuild the tier definitions around that input. The system now recaptures approximately 160 operational hours per year at better than 90% classification accuracy — and it ran without a single human intervention through a multi-month absence in early 2026. That operational continuity wasn't a feature I designed in isolation. It was the product of having built the classification model with the people whose judgment it was standing in for.
The same pattern held for the Executive RAG pipeline I built as a VP-level digital twin — a curated Voice DNA corpus plus a Kill List of rejected drafts that taught the agent risk tolerance and rhetorical pace through negative training. The 99% stylistic fidelity that system achieved didn't come from prompt engineering alone. It came from going back to the executive repeatedly with outputs and asking not just "is this right" but "where does this sound like someone who doesn't think the way you think" — and then building those corrections into the corpus as structured negative examples. The engineer's instinct to treat failure cases as data, not embarrassment, was something I absorbed from the environment and applied to my own craft.
The result, across the two years in that role, was a working pattern that I think is actually rare: a communications practitioner who can move into an engineer's frame of reference far enough to be useful at the design stage — not just the delivery stage. That's not the same as being an engineer. But it means the collaboration doesn't have to start with translation. It can start with the problem.
