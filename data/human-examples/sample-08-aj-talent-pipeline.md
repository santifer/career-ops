The brief I was handed at AJ+ in 2017 sounded like a production problem. It wasn't. It was a talent problem wearing a format problem as a disguise.
AJ+ at that point ran two parallel content lines. Context — Dena Takruri's team — produced 3-to-4-minute evergreen studio pieces with full research depth, releasing weekly. Real Time produced 1-minute social-native explainers with text-over-video and music, releasing daily. Both lines worked on their own terms. Neither could catch a breaking news story and explain it the same day. Ethar El-Katatney and Director Michael Shagoury asked me to design a third line that closed that gap: explainer-quality depth at real-time speed, same-day turnaround, 1-to-2 minutes. They also asked me to use that format as an on-camera talent pipeline — moving producers who had spent their careers behind the camera into presenter roles. Two problems, one format, one production line. That was the assignment.
The format design came first, and it was architectural before it was editorial. I built a day-of production logic that combined a breaking news hook with the research structure of a Context piece — a debunk or explanatory argument, supported by sourced B-roll, resolved in under two minutes. The structural constraint mattered: the 1-to-2 minute length forced every script to have exactly one throughline, which made it teachable. A producer who couldn't write a 4-minute documentary could write one argument with a setup, a pivot, and a payoff. That was the entry point for the coaching work.
Mara Van Ells was the first. She was a real-time producer — fast, algorithmically fluent, deeply comfortable behind the camera and completely unpracticed in front of it. I coached her through the proof-of-concept piece: how to carry a single argument across a tight script without losing pace, how to use the camera as a conversation rather than a performance, how to let the structure do the heavy lifting so the presentation didn't have to. Yara Elmjouie came next, and then Sana Saeed. Each had a different problem to solve — Yara's was register, learning to modulate between the analytical and the conversational inside a 90-second window; Sana's was authority, projecting sourced confidence on breaking material she'd had four hours to research. The format was the same for all three. The coaching was different every time.
The production outcome is documented. One video from that line hit 50 million views and 40,000 comments during AJ+'s peak digital-first era. But the outcome I track is the talent result: all three producers I coached directly — Mara, Yara, Sana — became on-camera principals with their own shows. All three went on to Emmy and Webby wins. I didn't make them good. They were already good. What I did was design a format that gave them a structured, low-stakes entry into a new capability, then stayed close enough through the first several pieces to catch the specific technical problems each one was solving — and name them precisely enough that the fix could stick.
That is the part that transfers. I've done a version of this work at every scale since: at Google's Office of Cross-Google Engineering, coaching Principal and Distinguished Engineers through the communications architecture behind agent deployment documentation; on the mentorship platform I rebuilt there, where the design question was how to systematize the knowledge transfer that had previously lived only in the heads of senior practitioners. The discipline is the same. You design a format that makes the skill learnable. You identify the specific breakdown for the specific person — not the general category of breakdown, the exact one. You name it with enough precision that they can recognize it themselves the next time. And you build the structure so it holds them long enough to internalize the logic, then gets out of the way.
The three producers who left AJ+ as on-camera principals didn't need a production manager. They needed someone who had thought carefully about what the format was actually asking of them — and could translate that into a coaching intervention precise enough to be useful on deadline, on a story that broke four hours ago, with a camera hot and a script that wasn't done yet.
