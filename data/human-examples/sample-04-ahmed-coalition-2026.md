The story begins in April 2026, on a Tuesday afternoon — a message from Ahmed Shihab-Eldin, the journalist and broadcaster I'd worked alongside during my Al Jazeera years, asking whether I could help coordinate a communications infrastructure for a coalition he was building around Kuwait. What followed was the kind of multi-stakeholder operation that doesn't announce its complexity upfront. It assembles itself around you in layers, and by the time you see the full shape of it, you're already responsible for making it work.
The coalition itself spanned principals across multiple geographies, institutions, and communication registers — people who needed to move in coordination without always being in the same room, on the same platform, or operating under the same assumptions about what could be said publicly and what needed to stay contained. That's the specific operational tension nobody's job description ever quite names: not the work of persuasion, and not the work of drafting. The work of architecture — deciding which information flows to which stakeholder, at what moment, through which channel, with what level of detail. Getting that wrong doesn't produce bad writing. It produces broken trust, or worse, exposed positions.
What I brought to it was a protocol instinct that runs through the whole career: the Fusion live specials where three competing broadcast organizations had to share editorial control in real time, the HuffPost Live Scientology coverage where every source booking moved under active multi-million-dollar litigation scrutiny, the xGE approvals governance overhaul where I imposed a Comment Freeze and a rejection standard requiring data-driven rationale — not because I wanted to slow things down, but because uncontrolled input at senior-IC scale produces noise that looks like signal. In each of those cases, the discipline wasn't caution for its own sake. It was the recognition that a coalition's operational security is only as strong as its least-considered communication decision. You don't protect a multi-stakeholder effort by drafting careful press releases. You protect it by designing the flow before anyone sends anything.
For Ahmed's coalition, that meant building a tiered coordination structure — separating the public-facing communications layer from the operational briefing layer, establishing clear escalation triggers for anything that moved from one tier to the other, and making sure the principals understood not just what to say but what the decision logic was behind the sequencing. The OPSEC discipline that shows up in my work — the same instinct that let me run autonomous triage infrastructure at Google that maintained 100% operational continuity through a multi-month absence without a single human intervention — is not a security-clearance mindset. It's a systems mindset. Information moves. The question is whether it moves by design or by accident.
The coalition held. The coordination infrastructure I built for it did what it was supposed to do: nobody said the wrong thing to the wrong audience at the wrong moment, the principals maintained coordinated public positioning across geographies, and the internal operational track stayed clean. I'm not going to manufacture a metric for it — this was relational and reputational capital, and its result was the absence of a failure rather than the presence of a number. That's a category of outcome that's genuinely hard to quantify, which is precisely why it tends to get undervalued by organizations that don't know what they're buying until they need it.
What I take from it is something I already knew but keep having confirmed: multi-stakeholder coordination at the policy-adjacent layer is a craft that looks like communication and operates like infrastructure. The visible output is messaging. The actual work is governance — who decides, who knows, who speaks, in what order, through what channel, with what accountability structure underneath it. I've been building that infrastructure, in one form or another, since the night Al Jazeera English went live to 250 million households and I had to abandon the entire planned rundown before the first segment aired. The names change. The discipline doesn't.
