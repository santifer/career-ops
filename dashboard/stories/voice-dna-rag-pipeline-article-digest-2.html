The problem wasn't speed. The problem was that the executive couldn't ship drafts that sounded like herself — and every cycle where she had to rewrite from scratch was a cycle where the actual work of running a horizontal engineering organization serving the top 0.5% of Google's 180,000-person workforce didn't get done.

When I joined the Office of Cross-Google Engineering as Internal Communications Lead, VP-level communications was burning time in the wrong direction. The drafting load was real — xGE's communications surface included everything from technical roadmap announcements to change-management narratives for company-wide engineering mandates — and the cycle-time pressure was compressing a process that depended on a quality signal that no generic prompt could replicate: the executive's voice. The way she framed risk. The rhetorical pace she preferred under pressure. The specific constructions she rejected so consistently that a pattern was legible in the rejections. That pattern was the data I needed.

I engineered the pipeline in two parallel tracks. The first was the Voice DNA corpus — a curated archive of legacy emails, executive-authored constitutions, and exemplar messaging drawn from her highest-stakes communications. This wasn't the generic document-dump approach to RAG context. Every item in the corpus was selected for signal density: pieces where the rhetorical fingerprint was clearest, where the executive had made distinctive choices under pressure rather than defaulting to organizational boilerplate. The second track was what I called the Kill List — a restricted corpus of rejected drafts, held separately, designed to teach the agent what the executive would not say. Negative training through accumulated refusals. The Kill List functioned as a boundary condition on the retrieval space, constraining the generation away from the stylistic territory she had already explicitly ruled out. Together, the two tracks gave the agent something closer to what a long-tenured chief of staff carries in their head: not just what the principal sounds like when she's on, but where she won't go.

The architecture underneath this was a RAG pipeline with the Voice DNA corpus and Kill List as separate retrieval layers — the Kill List applied as a constraint pass after initial draft generation rather than as direct retrieval context, so the agent was being shaped by the rejections without hallucinating their content into the output. The evaluation methodology was time-tracking on high-stakes drafting cycles before and after deployment, with fidelity assessed against the executive baseline across live communications rather than held-out test cases. Production, not prototype.

The result held at 99% stylistic fidelity relative to the executive baseline — the number that mattered, because at VP level the only fidelity that counts is the kind that lets the executive ship without rewriting from scratch. Drafting latency dropped 90%. The cycle-time compression that had been burning senior communications bandwidth released into the actual work of the organization.

What I understood building this — and what I'd want any applied AI team to know about how I think — is that voice fidelity at the VP level is not a cosmetic problem. It is an identity-under-pressure problem, and the agent has to learn the pressure signature, not just the surface register. The Kill List insight came from editorial instinct: in broadcast, you learn faster from what gets cut than from what makes air. The principle transferred exactly. The corpus design is the job. The architecture follows.
