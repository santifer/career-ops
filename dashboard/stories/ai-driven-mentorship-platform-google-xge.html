Translate emerging AI capabilities into practical team workflows

Source: OpenAI — Onboarding & Enablement Program Manager FDE · Humanize: 4% unknown

The mentorship program was breaking under its own weight. Not because the program itself was flawed — it was serving the top 0.5% of a 180,000-person engineering organization, matching Principal, Distinguished, and Fellow-level engineers with mentorship cohorts — but because every match was being processed by hand. Three and a half hours per match, administrative work eating into capacity, and a pipeline that couldn't scale to meet the demand that already existed. The system wasn't broken. It was manually operated when it didn't need to be.

My job was to change that. Not to rebuild the program's purpose or restructure its goals — those were working — but to identify exactly where the manual overhead was concentrated and replace it with something that could run without a human in the loop on every cycle.

The first move was diagnostic. I mapped the full matching workflow end to end, treating it the way I'd treat a live broadcast rundown: what are the inputs, what are the dependencies, where is time actually going? What I found was that the administrative processing time — intake, compatibility scoring, cohort assignment, confirmation routing — was the bottleneck, and it was almost entirely rule-bound. The matching logic wasn't discretionary. It was applying consistent criteria to structured inputs and producing structured outputs. That's not a task that requires human judgment on every instance. That's a task that requires human judgment once, when you design the criteria, and then systematic execution from there.

I architected the transition to an AI-driven internal platform that absorbed the rule-bound work and returned it to the program as capacity. The platform handled intake processing, compatibility matching against established criteria, and cohort assignment — the full administrative loop that had been running manually. I designed the handoff carefully. The goal wasn't to remove human judgment from the program; it was to remove human hands from the parts of the program where judgment had already been exercised and encoded into criteria. The distinction matters. When the system flags an edge case or surfaces a match that doesn't fit the standard criteria cleanly, it routes for human review. When it's executing against clear parameters, it executes. That architecture — autonomous on the rule-bound work, escalating on the ambiguous — is the same logic I'd used building the communications triage agent for the same organization: three-prompt routing, conditional escalation, documented rationale on every classification.

What the platform produced was a 90% reduction in administrative processing time per match. The 3.5-hour manual cycle dropped to under 20 minutes. That's not a marginal efficiency gain — that's a structural change in what the program can do. The capacity freed by that compression is what made a 300%-plus scaling in active deployment capacity for H1 2026 possible. The program that had been manually constrained now had room to grow into the demand that was already there.

The scale context matters here. Senior IC attrition at this tier doesn't behave like attrition at the population level. The estimated cost implications of each percentage point of senior engineer loss at a company of this size run to eight figures. A mentorship program at this level isn't a retention amenity — it's infrastructure. When I'm describing this work, I'm not describing a workflow improvement. I'm describing a system that needed to be reliable enough to function as infrastructure, which means it needed to be reliable enough to run without the brittleness of manual processing at every step.

What this project clarified for me is something I'd been operating on instinctively across the earlier work — at The Stream pulling live social signal into broadcast, at AJ+ designing the day-of explainer line that moved at real-time speed with context-team depth — which is that the most durable systems aren't the ones that automate everything. They're the ones that correctly identify which parts of the work are rule-bound and which parts require judgment, and build the architecture around that distinction. The AI didn't replace the program. It removed the friction that was keeping the program from operating at the scale it was already designed to reach.
