End-to-end ownership of major projects

Source: ElevenLabs — Forward Deployed Engineer - Software Engineer · Humanize: 9% low

The inbound queue for the Office of Cross-Google Engineering was a tax nobody had named yet. Roughly 1,000 senior technical ICs — Principal engineers, Distinguished engineers, Google Fellows, the top 0.5% of a 180,000-person engineering organization — were generating a steady stream of communications requests with no scalable triage layer between them and my calendar. Every request landed the same way: undifferentiated, unclassified, waiting. The operational cost was real, and I owned fixing it.

The architecture I built around that problem was a three-prompt autonomous agent — triage, revise, escalate — with conditional knowledge base loading keyed to request type. That sequencing was deliberate, not default. Triage runs first because classification is the only way to apply the right intervention at the right cost. A low-touch inbound request — a formatting question, a calendar-adjacent comms ask — doesn't need escalation logic; it needs Smart Brevity applied fast, with documented rationale, and a clean output the requester can ship. A high-touch request involving executive voice or reputational exposure needs something else entirely: a handoff with context, not a resolved draft. The three-prompt chain enforces that distinction programmatically, without asking a human to make the call each time.

The conditional KB loading was the part that took the most calibration. I wasn't building a general-purpose assistant — I was building a triage system for a specific population, operating inside a specific organizational context, where the cost of misclassification wasn't just wasted time. Routing a Medium request to the Low bin meant an executive-facing output went out without the right review layer. Routing a Low request to High meant I was the bottleneck again, just with extra steps. I tuned the classification thresholds against real inbound traffic, iterating on the boundary conditions between tiers until the accuracy held across the full stream. The system runs on a single Gemini agent — three-prompt routing, not multi-agent orchestration. That constraint was intentional. Complexity in agent architecture compounds failure modes; I kept the topology flat and the logic explicit.

End-to-end ownership of that system meant I held every layer: problem definition, architecture decision, prompt engineering, KB curation, classification calibration, deployment, and — critically — the operational continuity test I didn't plan for. During a multi-month medical leave of absence in early 2026, the triage agent maintained 100% operational continuity without a single human intervention. That is the production standard I built toward. Not "works when I'm watching it" — works when I'm gone. The system recaptured approximately 160 operational hours per year at greater than 90% classification accuracy across the full inbound stream. Those numbers are not projections from a pilot; they are the production deployment's measured output.

What I'd add to the metrics, because the metrics don't say it directly: the population this system serves carries an outsized organizational weight. Senior IC attrition at that tier carries estimated eight-figure cost implications per percentage point — retention infrastructure for that cohort isn't a nice-to-have. A triage agent that holds accurate, responsive, voice-faithful under the conditions that population generates is a different engineering problem than a generic comms bot. I built it for that population specifically, and it held. That's the proof of concept I'd want any forward deployed engineering context to understand before the interview starts.
