Develop messaging frameworks for technical + non-technical audiences

Source: Anthropic — Communications Lead, Claude Code Humanize: 2% low

The problem wasn't the VP's calendar. It was the drafts.

Every high-stakes communication that moved through the Office of Cross-Google Engineering — messaging to Principal, Distinguished, and Google Fellow engineers, the top 0.5% of a 180,000-person organization — had to carry the exact rhetorical fingerprint of the executive it came from. That's not a style preference. At that tier, voice is trust. A sentence that lands wrong with a Google Fellow isn't a style miss — it's a credibility event. I was writing for a VP-level principal under cycle-time pressure, producing communications for an audience of roughly 1,000 senior technical ICs who could parse a ghost-written email in two sentences. The drafting load was unsustainable. Something had to change architecturally.

What I built was a RAG pipeline I called Voice DNA — a digital twin for executive voice engineered specifically to survive the gap between the executive's instinct and what a fast drafting cycle actually produces. The first layer was a curated corpus: legacy emails, executive constitutions, exemplar high-stakes communications the principal had already shipped and considered representative. But positive examples alone don't teach a system where the edges are. So I added the Kill List — a structured archive of rejected drafts, annotated by failure mode, that taught the agent what the executive would never say and why. Rejected because too hedged. Rejected because the sentence buried the action. Rejected because the register was wrong for a Google Fellow audience that has no patience for throat-clearing. The Kill List is the part most people skip. It's also the part that accounts for the difference between a system that can approximate a voice and one that can hold it under pressure.

The technical architecture was a standard RAG retrieval layer — but the corpus curation and the negative-training logic were editorial decisions, not engineering ones. That's the distinction I'd make to anyone building this kind of system: the pipeline is a solved problem; what you put in it determines whether the output is a first draft or a ship-ready one. I drew the same instinct from a decade of newsroom production, where I'd spent years learning that the quality of the sourcing determines the quality of the segment before a single word is written. Voice DNA was the same logic applied to agent architecture: garbage in, approximate out. Curate the inputs with editorial discipline, and the system learns the risk tolerance instead of being told it.

The result was a 90% reduction in drafting latency on high-stakes communications cycles, with 99% stylistic fidelity relative to the executive baseline — measured against time-tracking data from drafting cycles before and after the RAG implementation. The VP could ship outputs without rewriting from scratch. I could move through a full communications cycle at a pace that matched the operational tempo of an engineering organization running company-wide mandate changes across all of Google, without sacrificing the voice fidelity that made those communications land with a technical IC audience that would notice immediately if they didn't.

What this built in me wasn't just a deployable technical artifact — it was a repeatable framework for the hardest translation problem in communications work: closing the gap between what a principal means and what an audience receives, across an audience that spans both technical and non-technical readers simultaneously. The VP's communications had to work for Distinguished Engineers debugging infrastructure decisions and for program managers reading update emails. Voice DNA didn't flatten that complexity — it held the executive voice stable while the content adapted. That's the same problem every communications framework for technical products has to solve: one voice, two audiences, no dilution in either direction.
