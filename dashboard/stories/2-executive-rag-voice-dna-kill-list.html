The VP I supported at Google's Office of Cross-Google Engineering was spending a disproportionate share of her cycle time on a single operational drag: every high-stakes communication had to clear her personally because no one else — human or machine — could produce a draft she'd ship without rewriting from scratch. The stylistic bar wasn't aesthetic preference. It was risk management. When you're communicating change mandates to a thousand Principal, Distinguished, and Google Fellow engineers — the top 0.5% of a 180,000-person org — a sentence that lands wrong isn't an awkward email. It's a leadership signal.

I'd watched enough drafts come back covered in tracked changes to understand what the real problem was. It wasn't that the drafts were bad. It was that the agent producing them — in this case, human staff using general-purpose tools — had no model of what "wrong" looked like for this executive. They had positive examples and guesswork. I had a different theory: the negative examples were the data. Every draft she'd rejected and rewritten over years in the role was a precise specification of her risk tolerance, her rhetorical pace, the constructions that felt like her and the ones that didn't. That archive wasn't a trash folder. It was a training set.

I engineered the RAG pipeline in two layers. The first was a "Voice DNA" corpus — curated from legacy executive emails, messaging frameworks she'd approved, and what I'd started thinking of as her editorial constitution: the decisions she made consistently under pressure. Not what she said she preferred in the abstract, but what she actually shipped when the stakes were real. The second layer was what I called the Kill List: a structured archive of rejected drafts, annotated by failure mode. A sentence that was technically accurate but stylistically flat. A framing that buried the ask. A hedged opening she'd cut every single time. The Kill List taught the pipeline what to avoid with the same precision the positive corpus taught it what to produce. Risk tolerance isn't a positive-space concept — you don't learn it from good examples alone. You learn it from the edge cases someone rejected without being able to fully articulate why.

What that two-layer architecture produced wasn't a draft generator. It was something closer to a digital twin — a system that had internalized the executive's voice well enough to produce outputs she could edit rather than rewrite. The operational result was a 90% reduction in drafting latency and 99% stylistic fidelity relative to her established baseline, measured against time-tracked drafting cycles before and after the pipeline went live. She stopped spending hours on first-pass drafts. The pipeline handled the first pass. She handled judgment.

What I take from this into any forward-deployed context isn't the specific architecture — RAG pipelines are table stakes now. It's the diagnostic instinct that got there. I didn't ask the executive what she wanted the system to produce. I watched what she kept rejecting, built a model of where her tolerance ended, and taught the agent from that boundary outward. That's the same move every time: get close enough to the actual workflow to see where the real friction lives, not where people think it does. The customer rarely describes the problem in terms that point directly to the solution. You have to be in the room — or close enough to it — to see what the drafts look like when they come back.
