Translating complex technical concepts

Source: Anthropic — Communications Manager, Research Humanize: 2% low

The problem wasn't the concept — it was the gap between what the system actually did and what anyone in the room could explain about it. I'd built a production-grade autonomous communications agent inside Google's Office of Cross-Google Engineering, serving roughly 1,000 of the company's most senior technical staff — Principal Engineers, Distinguished Engineers, Google Fellows, the top 0.5% of a 180,000-person organization. The system ran. The results were real. But the moment I had to explain the architecture to anyone outside the build, I watched the same thing happen every time: eyes that understood "AI" in the abstract and glazed over the instant the specifics arrived.

The agent ran on a three-prompt architecture — triage, revise, escalate — with conditional knowledge-base loading that triggered based on the classification tier it assigned to each inbound request. That sentence is technically accurate and functionally useless to most audiences. The architecture decision that mattered wasn't the three-prompt design; it was the reasoning behind it. The alternative was a single monolithic prompt trying to do all three jobs simultaneously, which in practice meant the model was always hedging — never fully committed to fast resolution, never fully committed to executive-voice fidelity when escalation was the right call. The three-prompt split forced commitment at each stage. Low-touch inbound got resolved with Smart Brevity applied and a documented rationale attached. High-touch inbound got flagged with enough context that a human could pick it up without re-reading the thread from the beginning. The system didn't just sort — it prepared.

Translating that to a non-technical audience meant abandoning the architecture language entirely and reaching for the newsroom analogy I knew worked: this is a wire editor. A wire editor at a broadcast outlet doesn't read every story that comes in at the same depth — they scan for urgency, route to the right desk, and draft the handoff note that lets the next person move without losing time. The triage agent was doing the same job. The conditional KB loading was the wire editor's institutional memory — knowing which stories the VP would touch personally and which ones could move without her. Once that frame landed, the conversation shifted from "how does the model work" to "what does the model know, and how did you teach it" — which was the question that actually mattered. The Kill List on the Voice DNA pipeline next door used the same translational logic: explaining a corpus of rejected drafts as a set of editorial red lines rather than negative training data made the capability legible to principals who'd never opened a fine-tuning doc in their lives.

The production numbers made the case once the concept landed. The triage system recaptured roughly 160 operational hours per year at greater than 90% classification accuracy across the full inbound stream. During a multi-month absence in early 2026, the system maintained 100% operational continuity without a single human intervention. That last metric is the one that travels — not because it's the largest number, but because it answers the question every executive actually wants answered: what happens when the person who built this isn't in the room.

What I've learned is that translating complex technical work isn't a communications problem — it's a modeling problem. You're not simplifying the concept; you're finding the domain where your audience already has the intuitions and mapping the unfamiliar thing onto it. A triage agent is a wire editor. A RAG pipeline with a curated corpus is institutional memory with a search layer. A conditional KB load is knowing which drawer to open without being asked. I spent a decade in broadcast learning to do this for international policy and legal complexity at two-minute runtime. The cognitive move is identical. The stakes inside an engineering organization are just structured differently.
