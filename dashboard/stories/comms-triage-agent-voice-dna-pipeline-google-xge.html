Build enablement infrastructure with AI tools embedded in workflows

Source: OpenAI — Onboarding & Enablement Program Manager FDE · Humanize: 5% unknown

The infrastructure problem at Google's Office of Cross-Google Engineering wasn't a communications problem — it was a scale problem wearing a communications problem's clothing. I was supporting roughly 1,000 senior technical ICs at the Principal, Distinguished, and Fellow tiers, and the inbound volume wasn't something you could hire your way through. The principals doing the most consequential engineering work in the organization were losing hours to request traffic that didn't require a human at all. The question wasn't whether to automate. The question was whether you could automate without the system breaking the moment a genuinely high-stakes request hit the queue.

The triage agent I built runs on a three-prompt architecture: triage, revise, escalate — in that order, with conditional knowledge base loading that fires based on what the first prompt finds. The routing logic sorts inbound traffic into Low, Medium, and High touch tiers, and the agent applies Smart Brevity formatting to the low-complexity subset before it surfaces anything to a human. That last detail matters more than it sounds. The instinct in most automation designs is to pass everything to a human and let the human decide what requires attention — which means you've built a slightly more organized inbox, not a triage system. The way this agent works, Low touch requests get drafted and documented with rationale before a human ever sees them. The human's first interaction with that traffic is a draft ready to approve or reject, not a raw request demanding a decision about what kind of request it is. The classification accuracy across the full inbound stream hit above 90%. That's not a rounding-error number — at the volume this system processes, a 90% classification rate means the human touch gets concentrated where it actually changes outcomes.

The second system I built for the same office attacked a different layer of the same problem. VP-level executive communications was eating cycle time in a different way — not volume, but fidelity. Ghostwriting under deadline pressure for a principal with a very specific rhetorical register is the kind of task that resists automation if you approach it wrong. The standard RAG approach — pull documents, generate against them, measure for hallucination — doesn't get you to voice fidelity. It gets you to content accuracy, which is a different problem. What I engineered instead was a two-corpus design: a "Voice DNA" corpus built from legacy emails, exemplar communications, and what I'd call executive constitutions — the documents where a principal has written out their own framework for how they think — paired with a "Kill List" of rejected drafts. The Kill List is the part that isn't obvious. Every rejected draft is a signal about risk tolerance and rhetorical boundary — where the executive's instinct says "that's not me" without necessarily being able to articulate why. Training the agent on that negative space alongside the positive corpus is what closes the gap between accurate and faithful. The result was a 90% reduction in drafting latency and 99% stylistic fidelity relative to the executive's established baseline, measured across high-stakes drafting cycles before and after the RAG deployment.

The operational continuity result is the one I keep coming back to. When I was out on a multi-month medical leave beginning in early 2026, both systems ran without a single human intervention. Not reduced performance — 100% operational continuity across the full absence. That's the real test of whether you've built infrastructure or a demo. A demo requires the builder in the room. Infrastructure runs when the builder isn't.

What the triage agent and the Voice DNA pipeline together represent is something I'd describe as embedded enablement — AI capability that's genuinely in the workflow, not adjacent to it. The distinction is architectural: a tool you use is something you choose to open; infrastructure you've embedded is something that's already made a decision by the time you arrive. For an organization serving the top 0.5% of a 180,000-person engineering workforce, where each percentage point of senior IC attrition carries estimated eight-figure cost implications, the value of keeping that talent's attention on engineering rather than administrative friction isn't an efficiency metric — it's a retention argument. That's the context in which I built these systems, and it's the context that shaped every design decision. The audience demanded it work. The stakes demanded it hold.
