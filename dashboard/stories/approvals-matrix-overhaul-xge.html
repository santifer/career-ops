The approvals process at xGE wasn't broken in a dramatic way. It was broken in the way that death-by-a-thousand-cuts is broken — a steady accumulation of ad hoc executive requests, subjective feedback loops with no data behind them, and a governance structure so informal that "approval" meant whatever the most recent stakeholder in the thread wanted it to mean. No one had named the problem. Everyone had learned to work around it.

My job was to stop working around it and fix it instead.

The first thing I did was map the actual flow of decisions rather than the assumed one. What I found was that the formal approval chain and the real approval chain were different documents. Requests were arriving through multiple channels simultaneously — email, Slack, direct calendar drops — with no intake standard, no criteria for what constituted a complete request, and no common definition of what "rejected" meant. The result was predictable: low-value requests got the same executive attention as high-stakes ones, feedback arrived as opinion rather than rationale, and the revision cycle had no defined terminus. Work that should have closed in two days was running open for two weeks.

I designed a three-part governance overhaul. The first piece was an Approvals Matrix — a structured decision framework that mapped request type to approval tier, clarified ownership at each tier, and eliminated the ambiguity that was letting every stakeholder treat their lane as a veto lane. The matrix wasn't a bureaucratic artifact; it was a forcing function. If a request didn't fit a defined category, that was diagnostic information about the request, not a reason to route it upward for an executive judgment call. The second piece was a 7-day Comment Freeze — a hard window that consolidated feedback cycles and killed the informal drip of late-arriving revisions that had been extending timelines indefinitely. If your feedback didn't arrive during the freeze window, it didn't reopen the cycle. The third piece was the most culturally difficult: a standing rejection of subjective feedback lacking data-driven rationale. "I don't like the tone" without a documented standard wasn't feedback — it was noise. I pushed the organization toward a norm where revision requests required a stated basis, which meant stakeholders had to articulate what they were actually asking for before they could slow the process down.

Getting those three pieces adopted required working across Legal, Finance, Product, and Engineering simultaneously — not sequentially. Each function had its own compliance posture and its own definition of acceptable risk in the approval process. Legal wanted documentation trails. Finance wanted budget-impact tagging. Product wanted speed. Engineering wanted criteria, not judgment calls. The matrix had to satisfy all four simultaneously, which meant the design process was a negotiation before it was a governance document. I held stakeholder alignment meetings with representatives from each function, drafted and re-drafted the criteria language until it held up under cross-functional scrutiny, and ran a parallel internal communication effort so that the rollout wasn't a surprise to the IC population that would live inside the new framework. Change-management communication was built into the project from the start, not bolted on at the end.

The result was a 50% reduction in low-value, ad hoc executive requests — a durable outcome, not a temporary suppression effect. The matrix held under operational load. The Comment Freeze became a standard expectation rather than a contested policy. The feedback-rationale requirement shifted the default posture of stakeholders before they entered the process, not just during it.

What I keep thinking about is what the overhaul was actually solving for. The surface problem was approval latency. The real problem was that without defined criteria and enforced process boundaries, every decision was being made twice — once formally and once informally — and the informal layer was doing most of the damage. Governance frameworks aren't interesting as documents. They're interesting as the infrastructure that lets high-stakes work move at the speed the work requires. That's the same instinct I bring to agentic system design: the triage architecture, the escalation threshold, the conditional loading logic. It's all the same problem. Define the criteria before the pressure arrives, and the system can run without you.
