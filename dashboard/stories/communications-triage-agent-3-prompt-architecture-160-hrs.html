Prototype → production deployment

The request that kicked off the Communications Triage Agent wasn't a product brief. It was a capacity problem with no clean ceiling — roughly 1,000 Principal, Distinguished, and Fellow engineers across the Office of Cross-Google Engineering generating inbound communications traffic that required human judgment to sort, and exactly one person whose job it was to make those calls. Every low-complexity request that landed in the queue ate time that belonged to something harder.

The architecture I built to solve it is a three-prompt chain: triage, revise, escalate — in that order, with conditional knowledge base loading at the triage stage. The agent reads the inbound request, classifies it into one of three touch tiers, and routes accordingly. Low-touch traffic — the bulk of the volume — gets resolved autonomously, with Smart Brevity applied to the output and the classification rationale logged. Medium-touch requests get a drafted revision with flagged decision points. High-touch traffic gets escalated with a structured brief, not a raw forward. The conditional KB loading matters here in a way that's easy to understate: the agent doesn't load the full knowledge base on every pass. It pulls only what's relevant to the classification, which keeps the context window tight and the output precise rather than comprehensive. That distinction — precision over comprehensiveness — is what separates a production communications tool from a demo that looks good until it hits a real edge case.

What made the architecture work wasn't the three-prompt structure itself. Plenty of people can sketch a triage → revise → escalate flow on a whiteboard. What made it production-grade was the calibration work that preceded deployment: mapping the actual inbound taxonomy from historical traffic, defining what "Low touch" meant in a population where the requesters are Principal and Distinguished engineers who write precise questions, and building the escalation brief format so that when something did require human judgment, the human was handed a structured decision rather than a pile of context. The kill conditions were as important as the routing logic. The agent needed to know not just what it could handle — but what it absolutely shouldn't, and how to hand that off without losing the thread.

The system went into production. Not a pilot, not a staged rollout with human review on every output — production, running unattended against a live queue of senior engineering stakeholders. The numbers that came out of that deployment: approximately 160 operational hours recaptured per year, at greater than 90% classification accuracy across the full inbound stream. Those figures held. When a multi-month medical leave took me out of the picture in early 2026, the triage framework maintained 100% operational continuity — no single human intervention required. A system that runs accurately while you're watching it is a prototype. A system that runs accurately while you're absent for months is infrastructure.

The part of this I keep coming back to — and the part I think gets undersold when people talk about AI deployment in enterprise comms — is that the accuracy ceiling in a system like this isn't primarily a model problem. The model was capable. The ceiling was definitional: how precisely had I mapped the inbound categories, how cleanly had I separated what the agent was authorized to resolve versus flag, and how disciplined was the KB curation that grounded those decisions. That's editorial work dressed in systems language. It's the same instinct I spent a decade building in live broadcast — knowing exactly how much you can decide on your own, and exactly where the call has to go upstairs. The triage agent is that instinct, operationalized.
