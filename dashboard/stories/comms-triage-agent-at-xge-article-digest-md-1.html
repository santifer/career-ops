Production agent shipping at scale

Source: Pinecone — Staff Developer Advocate Humanize: 5% low

The communications volume inside xGE was structural, not incidental. The Office of Cross-Google Engineering supports roughly 1,000 senior technical ICs — Principal, Distinguished, and Google Fellow engineers, the top 0.5% of a 180,000-person organization — and inbound requests arrived with no triage layer between the sender and a human calendar. Every low-complexity ask consumed the same attention as a high-stakes executive communication. The system had no memory of its own throughput, no routing logic, no way to distinguish a 90-second resolution from a two-hour one. I built one.

The agent runs on a three-prompt architecture: triage, revise, escalate. That sequence is not arbitrary — it mirrors the actual decision tree a senior comms operator runs in their head when inbound traffic hits. The first prompt classifies. It reads the request against a conditional knowledge base that loads selectively depending on what the triage prompt surfaces — not a static dump of every policy document the org has ever produced, but a scoped retrieval that pulls only what the classification warrants. Low-touch requests get Smart Brevity treatment: the agent drafts a response at the appropriate register, documents its rationale in a structured log, and resolves without escalation. Medium-touch requests get a revised draft with flagged assumptions held for light human review. High-touch requests — anything touching executive voice at the VP level, anything with political valence inside the org, anything the confidence score doesn't clear — escalate to me with context pre-loaded. The human only enters where the human is actually needed.

The craft decision I'm most deliberate about is the conditional KB loading. The failure mode for most retrieval-augmented triage systems is retrieval sprawl — the agent pulls too much, dilutes its own signal, and produces responses that are technically grounded but tonally inconsistent. I scoped the knowledge base by tier and by request type, so the agent is never reasoning against the full corpus when a narrow slice is sufficient. That discipline is what holds the classification accuracy at scale. The other deliberate choice was the documented rationale on resolved requests — the agent writes a one-line reasoning note for every low-touch resolution it handles autonomously. That log exists for auditability, but it also functions as calibration data: I can review a week's resolved volume, identify where the agent's confidence was high but its reasoning was off, and adjust the triage prompt accordingly without retraining from scratch. It's a feedback loop that runs on the same infrastructure as the agent itself.

The result is documented in the CV and worth stating precisely: approximately 160 operational hours recaptured per year at greater than 90% classification accuracy across the full inbound stream. Those aren't prototype numbers — that's production throughput against a live principal population where a misclassification carries real reputational cost. The more significant proof point is the continuity test: during a multi-month medical leave of absence in early 2026, the triage system maintained 100% operational continuity without a single human intervention. The organization did not degrade. The queue did not back up. The senior IC cohort did not notice I was gone — which is exactly what a production agent is supposed to do.

What I keep returning to is how much the editorial instinct and the engineering instinct are the same instinct. A live broadcast control room is a triage system. You have inbound signal from a dozen sources simultaneously, a confidence threshold on every piece of it, and a decision architecture that routes low-complexity inputs to the B-block and escalates breaking news to the top of the rundown without convening a committee. The comms triage agent is that control room — formalized, automated, and running unattended. The years in broadcast didn't prepare me for this work. They were this work, in a different infrastructure.
