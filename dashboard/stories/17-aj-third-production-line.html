The format problem at AJ+ wasn't obvious from the outside. From the inside, it was a structural gap you could fall through every day.

AJ+ ran two parallel content lines in 2016 and 2017. Context — the in-house explainer group I'd been hired into — produced deep, researched pieces: three to four minutes, studio-polished, evergreen framing, weekly cadence. Dena Takruri's electoral college work was the canonical output of that line. Real Time ran the opposite direction: one-minute social-native clips, text-over-video, music bed, same-day turnaround, built for the scroll. Both lines worked on their own terms. Neither could catch a breaking story and explain it in the same breath. When something genuinely complex broke fast — a Senate vote, a climate report, a protest crackdown — you either had speed without depth or depth that arrived three days late. AJ+ leadership, Ethar El-Katatney and Director Michael Shagoury, asked me to design a third line that answered both questions at once.

The format I built was a day-of explainer: a breaking news hook processed through context-team research depth, structured as a two-minute on-camera piece with full B-roll, sourced argument, and a clear analytical frame — shipped the same day. The technical constraint was brutal and clarifying. You couldn't hit that turnaround with the Context team's production cadence, so the piece had to be architecturally simpler: one presenter, one clear thesis, one through-line of evidence, no studio setup that required a full crew call. What looked like a creative format decision was actually a systems decision — I was designing for throughput without sacrificing the explanatory integrity that made AJ+ different from the Real Time competitors. The structural middle ground between two existing lines isn't a compromise. It's a new category, and the format discipline had to be tight enough that a producer working under deadline pressure could execute it reliably, not just on a good day.

The second half of the mandate was the part that made the production line genuinely hard to build: I was supposed to use the format as an on-camera talent pipeline. Real Time producers were strong researchers and fast operators, but they'd built their careers entirely behind the camera. The proof-of-concept piece meant coaching Mara Van Ells from producer to presenter — not just giving her camera time, but designing an on-ramp where the format's structural constraints actually made the transition easier. A two-minute piece with a clear thesis and a tight research package gave a first-time presenter something to hold onto. The format was scaffolding before it was a showcase. I subsequently brought Yara Elmjouie and Sana Saeed through the same pipeline. All three went on to become on-camera principals with their own shows; the Emmy and Webby wins in their trajectories aren't mine to claim, but the system that gave them the first on-camera reps is documented.

The video I produced during that period that best stress-tested the third-line format hit 50 million views and 40,000 comments — the high-water mark of AJ+'s digital-first era. That number is a reach metric, not a quality metric, but reach at that scale on an explainer-format piece is evidence that depth and speed aren't actually in tension when the architecture is right. AJ+ was the second-largest news video producer on Facebook at its peak. That context matters: the format I was operating inside wasn't marginal. It was the core of what made AJ+ the category it was, and the third line was designed to extend that category's range, not decorate it.

What I understand now, looking back at that build, is that the constraint structure was the whole thing. Every decision I made — turnaround time, piece length, presenter scaffolding, B-roll discipline — was a systems decision dressed up as a creative one. The format had to be fast enough to ship the same day, simple enough for a first-time presenter to execute under deadline, and structured enough to produce explanatory depth without a studio infrastructure that couldn't move at that speed. That's the same problem I solve now when I'm building an agent: identify the constraint surface, design the architecture around it, make the first version ship-able in days, and then harden the format — or the system — into something that runs reliably without you holding it up. The AJ+ third line wasn't a prototype. It was a production deployment. The talent it produced kept running long after I was gone.
