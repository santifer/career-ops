The hardest thing about writing for a VP isn't the words. It's the risk tolerance — knowing which argument they'd never make in public, which rhetorical move they'd find overclaimed, which concession they'd accept before the sentence even finished. Style guides don't capture that. Tone references don't either. I built a system that did.

In the Office of Cross-Google Engineering, I was writing communications for VP-level principals serving roughly 1,000 of Google's most-senior technical ICs — Principal, Distinguished, and Fellow engineers, the top 0.5% of a 180,000-person organization. The drafting pressure was real: high-stakes messages on engineering mandate changes, technical roadmap shifts, senior IC retention signaling. The cycle-time pressure was equally real. Every round of executive revision burned time that didn't exist, and stylistic drift — a phrase too hedged, a sentence too long, a framing the executive would never use — meant the draft went back regardless of how technically correct it was.

The standard approach to executive ghostwriting is proximity. You absorb the principal's voice over time, you develop editorial instinct, you get better. That works at a certain scale. It doesn't work when the pipeline is running at volume and the executive can't afford to be the quality-control layer on every outgoing message. I needed something structurally faster — not just faster to write, but faster to be right. So I engineered the Voice DNA RAG pipeline: a retrieval-augmented generation system built on a curated corpus of legacy emails, executive communications, and exemplar messaging, trained through what I called a Kill List — a restricted bank of rejected drafts that taught the agent not just what the executive would say, but what they'd rejected, and why. The Kill List is the piece most implementations miss. Any RAG pipeline can surface stylistic patterns from approved output. Negative training — explicitly ingesting the rhetorical moves the principal found overclaimed, the framings they'd cut, the sentence structures they'd rewritten — is what calibrates risk tolerance. That's not the same as style. It's editorial judgment encoded into retrieval architecture. The distinction matters at VP level, where the difference between a message that ships and a message that comes back for revision lives entirely in that register.

The result was a 90% reduction in drafting latency and 99% stylistic fidelity relative to the executive baseline — measured against time-tracked high-stakes drafting cycles before and after the pipeline went into production. Those aren't prototype numbers. The system ran in production, unattended, at the volume the role required.

What I understood building that system — and what I'd understood long before the technology existed to build it — is that tone adaptation isn't a single-axis problem. General audiences and expert audiences don't just need different vocabulary. They need different implied assumptions about what requires explanation, different calibrations of what counts as evidence, different judgments about when to acknowledge complexity and when to resolve it. At HuffPost Live, I was assembling five-expert panels on PrEP clinical trials — booking the IPREX trial chair from Lima, Peru, months after FDA approval — and making that accessible to a live audience with no medical background, in real time, with no edit buffer. At AJ+, I was translating Senate lobbying mechanics and ACLU First Amendment analysis into under two-and-a-half minutes of social video without losing the argument. At xGE, I was writing technical roadmap communications for engineers who would immediately identify any imprecision, in a voice that had to sound like one person, every time. These aren't three different skills. They're the same skill applied at different registers — the ability to hold the expert and the general audience simultaneously and make a judgment, sentence by sentence, about which demands more attention right now.

The Voice DNA pipeline made that judgment systematic. What had previously required editorial instinct built over years of proximity to a principal — knowing their risk tolerance, their rhetorical pace, their tolerance for ambiguity — became something a production system could execute at speed. That's the work I find most interesting: not just writing well across registers, but building the infrastructure that lets precision travel at scale.
