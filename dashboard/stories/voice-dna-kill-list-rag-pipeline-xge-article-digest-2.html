The problem wasn't that the VP's drafts were bad. The problem was that every high-stakes communications cycle—technical roadmap announcements, engineering mandate shifts, internal messaging to the top 0.5% of Google's engineering organization—was consuming hours of senior drafting time to produce outputs that still needed to sound like one person. At that velocity, stylistic fidelity isn't a nice-to-have. It's the entire product.

I was the Internal Communications Lead inside Google's Office of Cross-Google Engineering, a horizontal organization tasked with driving technical change across all of Google and running enablement and mentorship programs for roughly 1,000 Principal, Distinguished, and Google Fellow engineers. The communications throughput that role demanded was significant—not in volume alone, but in precision. The people receiving these communications could tell immediately when something had been drafted by committee or generated from a generic template. They built systems for a living. They recognized scaffolding.

The architecture I built to solve this isn't complicated to describe, but the craft is in what you feed it. A RAG pipeline—retrieval-augmented generation—is only as good as the corpus you curate for it. I built two distinct data layers. The first was the "Voice DNA" corpus: legacy emails, executive constitutions, high-stakes messaging the VP had written and shipped, exemplar communications across different registers and audiences. The second layer was what I called the Kill List—a deliberately curated set of rejected drafts, marked up with notes on why they failed. Not wrong facts, not incomplete arguments, but stylistic misses: the wrong rhetorical pace, a hedge where the executive would have cut, a softening phrase that read as institutional caution rather than directness. Most RAG implementations train on what good looks like. The Kill List taught the agent what this specific principal would not say, which is a different and harder problem. Risk tolerance is communicated through refusals as much as through approvals.

The result held up at measurement. Drafting latency dropped 90% on the high-stakes cycles where I had before-and-after time tracking in place. Stylistic fidelity reached 99% against the executive baseline—not a self-assessed number, but one validated against drafting cycles where the executive's edits to RAG outputs were tracked as a delta from baseline. On the most important communications this office produced, the gap between what the pipeline generated and what the executive would have written from scratch became operationally negligible. That's the standard: not "good enough to fix," but "good enough to ship."

What this story is actually about—and why I lead with it in conversations about customer-embedded and strategic-partner work—is that the technical architecture is the easier half. The harder half is the embedded trust that has to precede it. Building a digital twin for a VP-level communicator means you have spent enough time inside their thinking to know which rejected draft was rejected for a values reason versus a prose reason. You have to be close enough to the principal to curate the Kill List at all. The pipeline didn't work because the engineering was sophisticated. It worked because I had built, over time, the kind of embedded operating relationship with that executive where I understood the difference between a risk they would accept and one they wouldn't—and could encode that distinction into a system that ran without me in the room. That's not a technical deliverable. That's strategic-partner work. The RAG pipeline was just the artifact it produced.
