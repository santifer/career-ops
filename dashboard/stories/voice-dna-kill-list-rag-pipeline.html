Improving pieces without losing essence

Source: Anthropic — Engineering Editorial Lead · Humanize: 11% low

The problem wasn't that the VP's drafts were bad. The problem was that every draft that came back from a generic LLM was *almost* right — and almost right, at VP level, is its own category of wrong. A revision cycle on a near-miss burns more time than starting from scratch. That's the gap I was hired to close.

I was Internal Communications Lead inside Google's Office of Cross-Google Engineering — xGE, the horizontal engineering organization driving technical change across all of Google. The audience for everything my office produced was the top 0.5% of a 180,000-person engineering organization: Principal Engineers, Distinguished Engineers, Google Fellows. These are not people who let a wrong word pass without comment. Every piece of communication that left the VP's office needed to land as if she had written it herself, not as if someone had approximated her.

The gap wasn't effort — it was architecture. Standard RAG pipelines treat a voice like a template: give the model enough examples and it will pattern-match to the surface. The problem is that surface fidelity is not voice fidelity. A writer's rhetorical signature isn't just the words she chooses; it's the risks she's willing to take, the framings she has learned — sometimes painfully — to reject. I'd seen this problem from the other side for years. At AJ+, I coached three real-time producers into on-camera presenting roles. What I learned then is what I applied now: you can't teach someone a voice by showing them only what's correct. You have to show them what's been refused.

That's the design logic behind what I built — a two-corpus RAG pipeline. The first corpus was Voice DNA: legacy executive emails, exemplar messaging, what I came to think of as the executive's "constitution" — the sentences that had shipped without a single revision, the structural moves she returned to when the stakes were highest. The second corpus was the Kill List: rejected drafts, flagged phrases, the near-misses that had come back with margin notes. The Kill List taught the agent risk tolerance and rhetorical pace through negative training — not just what the voice sounds like, but where the voice will not go. Most voice-calibration systems I'd seen were trained entirely on positive examples. That's like trying to learn a legal standard only from winning cases. The losing cases are where the line actually lives.

The engineering work was precise: curating the corpora required editorial judgment at every step. A rejected draft isn't instructive on its own — you need to know *why* it was rejected, which meant tagging and annotating each Kill List entry with the specific failure mode it represented. Too hedged. Too promotional. Wrong level of technical granularity for a Fellow-tier audience. Rhetorical pace too slow — she accelerates through setup, lands hard on the verb, doesn't linger. Each annotation became a negative training signal the agent could apply to new drafts before output, not after review.

The results were measurable in two registers. Operationally: 90% reduction in drafting latency on high-stakes cycles, measured against the time-tracking we ran on the same class of deliverables before and after RAG implementation. Qualitatively — and this is the number I consider the harder one to earn: 99% stylistic fidelity relative to the executive baseline. That figure came from a structured comparison of RAG-assisted output against the executive's unassisted drafts across a defined set of message types. The pipeline wasn't producing drafts that needed to be rebuilt. It was producing drafts that needed to be *sent*.

The part that stays with me is that this problem wasn't really a technical problem. It was an editorial problem that required a technical solution — and the editorial instinct had to come first. Every decision about what went into the Voice DNA corpus, which rejected drafts made the Kill List and why, how to annotate the failure modes so the agent could apply them generatively — those were news-judgment calls wearing different clothes. I'd spent a decade learning how to preserve what matters in a piece while cutting everything that doesn't. The Voice DNA pipeline is that same discipline made into infrastructure. The essence isn't in the approved examples. It's in understanding, precisely, what you won't approve — and building a system that knows the difference before you have to say it.
