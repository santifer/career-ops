"World-class writing, fast tempo"

Source: Perplexity — Executive Communications Manager (Sr Manager, Exec Comms) Humanize: 5% low

The brief from AJ+ leadership was deceptively simple: build a content format that could answer breaking news with explanatory depth, same day, every day. What they were actually asking for was a production line that didn't exist — not in their shop, not anywhere. Context team pieces ran three to four minutes and took a week. Real-time pieces ran a minute and took hours but traded depth for speed. The gap between them was where the audience actually lived, and nobody had built infrastructure to reach it.

I was hired onto the Context team as a Senior Producer. The real-time line was producing fast and shallow. Context was producing slow and deep. AJ+ leadership — Ethar and Director Michael Shagoury — asked me to design a third line that did both at once: explainer-quality depth at same-day turnaround, inside a two-minute format built for platform distribution. The assignment had a second requirement embedded inside it: use the format as a talent pipeline. Move real-time producers from behind-camera roles into on-camera presenter roles. Build the format and the people simultaneously.

The format problem was an architecture problem first. A two-minute explainer that actually explains something isn't just a shorter version of a longer piece — it's a different structure entirely. The opening has to double as the hook and the thesis. The argument has to carry B-roll that would survive a scroll. The pacing has to be calibrated to the platform's attention economy, not to broadcast rhythm. I designed the production line around a day-of discipline: identify the breaking hook by morning, lock the argument by early afternoon, shoot and cut same day. No multi-day production cycles. The format forced every editorial decision to be made under real-time pressure, which meant the research had to be done before the camera was ever involved — the explainer depth had to be front-loaded into prep, not discovered in post.

The talent-pipeline layer required a different kind of engineering. Taking a producer who was skilled and confident behind a camera and putting them in front of one isn't a direction change — it's a trust architecture problem. I coached Mara Van Ells from her behind-camera production role into the on-camera presenter position for the proof-of-concept piece. Then Yara Elmjouie. Then Sana Saeed. Each required a different approach to the same underlying problem: how do you preserve what a producer already knows how to do — the structural thinking, the editorial instinct, the argument discipline — and translate it into a format the camera can read? The answer was to build the coaching around the format itself. The two-minute structure gave them a scaffold. Once they could feel the architecture of the piece, the camera stopped being an obstacle.

The result was a format that scaled in two directions at once. One video in that line hit 50 million views. That's the output number. The talent output is the more durable metric: three producers I coached directly — Mara Van Ells, Yara Elmjouie, Sana Saeed — went on to become on-camera principals with their own shows, including Emmy and Webby wins. The production line built the format and the people at the same time, which was the original requirement, and it did it at the speed of breaking news, which was the harder one.

What I understand now that I didn't have language for then is that this was a systems-design problem wearing a content problem's clothes. The format was the interface. The production discipline was the infrastructure. The talent coaching was the deployment layer. Every constraint — the two-minute window, the same-day turnaround, the on-camera requirement — was a forcing function that made the system more precise. That's the instinct I've carried forward: the pressure doesn't degrade the work. If the architecture is right, pressure is what makes it ship.
