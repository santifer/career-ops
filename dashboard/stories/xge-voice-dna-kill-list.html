Translate domain expert work into compelling narratives

Source: ElevenLabs — Communications Manager · Humanize: 2% low

The problem wasn't a slow pipeline. It was a fidelity problem dressed up as a speed problem — and most people trying to solve it were solving the wrong one.

When I joined the Office of Cross-Google Engineering, the VP-level communications workload was grinding. xGE is a horizontal engineering organization: it drives technical change across all of Google, manages roadmap shifts, runs change-management communications for company-wide engineering mandates, and operates the principal leadership programs for roughly 1,000 engineers at the Principal, Distinguished, and Fellow tiers — the top 0.5% of a 180,000-person organization. The volume of executive communications that moved through that office was significant, but the real friction wasn't the volume. It was that every high-stakes draft required the executive to rewrite it from scratch because the model couldn't hold the voice. Drafts would come back structurally correct and rhetorically inert — the kind of output that reads fine in isolation and reads wrong the moment the executive touches it. The cycle time bleed wasn't in the drafting. It was in the revision loop that followed every draft that missed.

The standard approach to this problem is to feed the model more examples and hope for emergent calibration. I went the other direction. The core engineering insight was that a voice is defined as much by its refusals as by its patterns — what the writer won't say, won't allow, and has already killed is at least as informative as what survived to publication. I built the RAG pipeline in two distinct layers. The first was the Voice DNA corpus itself: curated legacy emails, approved executive messaging, exemplar communications that had landed cleanly — documents that captured rhetorical pace, risk tolerance, the specific register this particular executive used when delivering technical constraint to a senior engineering audience versus when addressing program milestones. That corpus established the affirmative pattern. The second layer was the Kill List: a restricted corpus of rejected drafts, flagged revisions, and explicitly discarded framings that taught the pipeline what the executive had already decided was off-limits. Not wrong in the abstract — wrong for this voice, this context, this risk posture. The Kill List operationalized something that stylistic training almost never captures: the editorial reflex that stops a draft before it ships. Together, the two layers gave the agent a functional model of the executive's judgment, not just the executive's vocabulary.

The implementation required discipline about what went into each corpus layer. An undiscriminating Kill List would contaminate the positive signal; a Kill List built only on catastrophic failures would miss the subtler refusals that define a mature editorial voice. I curated both sides manually — the work was archival and analytical before it was technical. The pipeline itself ran on standard RAG architecture, but the corpus construction methodology was where the actual differentiation lived. The resulting system didn't simulate the voice; it reproduced the executive's decision-making posture at the drafting layer.

The measured result: 90% reduction in drafting latency on high-stakes communications cycles, with 99% stylistic fidelity to the executive baseline — measured against the before/after time tracking on the revision loop, not against an internal benchmark I designed to pass. The executive could ship outputs without rebuilding them. That's the operational definition of fidelity at this tier. For a senior technical leadership communications function inside a company where each percentage point of senior IC attrition carries eight-figure cost implications, the value isn't in the hours recovered. It's in the quality floor the system maintains under deadline pressure — the communications that land correctly the first time, when the audience is Google's most-senior engineers and the stakes of a wrong tone are organizational.

The deeper lesson wasn't about RAG architecture. It was about what it actually means to translate a domain expert's voice into a system that can speak on their behalf. The translation work isn't linguistic — it's editorial. You have to understand the expert's risk posture before you can build the corpus, and you have to understand what they've already rejected before you can reliably replicate what they'd approve. That's the same instinct I've used in every communications context I've operated in — the difference between a story that lands and one that requires explanation is almost always in the framing decisions that happened before anyone touched a keyboard. The Voice DNA pipeline automated that instinct. The Kill List gave it teeth.
