Build repeatable playbooks from field learnings

Source: Anthropic — Manager, Forward Deployed Engineering · Humanize: 2% low

The problem wasn't the volume of requests. It was the texture of them — ad hoc, undifferentiated, arriving without context or triage, each one consuming the same senior-staff attention regardless of whether it warranted thirty seconds or three hours. When I took on the Internal Communications Lead role inside Google's Office of Cross-Google Engineering, the approval and review workflows for a population of roughly 1,000 Principal, Distinguished, and Fellow engineers operated on the same informal basis they always had: whoever asked loudest got a response, subjective feedback moved through unchecked, and there was no mechanism for distinguishing a routine request from a genuine escalation. The result was exactly what you'd expect — a slow accumulation of low-value work that nobody had formally authorized and nobody had formally rejected.

The first thing I built wasn't a system. It was a decision about what the system needed to refuse. Governance frameworks fail not because they lack rules but because they lack teeth on the rejection side — they tell people how to submit work without ever telling them what disqualifies it. So before I touched intake or routing, I established the standard: subjective feedback lacking data-driven rationale would be sent back. Full stop. That single clause — call it the rejection rule — changed the character of incoming requests faster than any intake form would have. People stopped submitting gut-feel asks when they knew the response would be a request for evidence. The 7-day Comment Freeze followed the same logic: it forced consolidation before anything reached review, which meant the requests that did arrive were already pre-filtered for the things the submitter was actually confident about. Not everything, not a hedge — the things they'd commit to in writing after a week of sitting with it.

The Approvals Matrix was the third layer, and the one that codified the first two into something repeatable without me in the loop. I built it to answer a question that governance processes almost never answer explicitly: who has authority to approve what, and at what threshold does something escalate versus resolve at the current tier? Mapping that — actually writing it down, assigning names and scope, defining the escalation criteria — made visible a set of implicit assumptions that had been operating as informal norms for years. Some of those norms were fine. Others were the source of the ad hoc executive requests that were eating capacity. The matrix didn't eliminate judgment; it relocated judgment to the right altitude. A routine request could resolve at the program level without touching VP bandwidth. An actual escalation had a defined path to the right desk with the right framing already attached.

The result was a 50% reduction in low-value, ad hoc executive requests — documented in xGE's operational tracking after the governance overhaul. That number matters less as a metric than as a signal: the system was doing the filtering work that had previously been done by whichever senior IC happened to be in the path of an incoming ask. The three pieces — the rejection rule, the freeze, the matrix — didn't require any of them to be technically sophisticated. They required being precise about what the process was trying to protect, and then building the minimum structure that protected it without adding bureaucratic mass on top.

What I took from this into every subsequent piece of systems design — including the autonomous triage agent I later built to classify inbound communications for that same population — is that a playbook isn't a procedure document. A procedure document describes what to do. A playbook describes what to do, what disqualifies something from proceeding, and who owns which decision at which altitude. The xGE governance work taught me to write the rejection criteria first. Everything else follows from knowing what the system is allowed to say no to.
