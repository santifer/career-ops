The mentorship program I inherited wasn't broken. It was manually intensive in a way that had become structurally incompatible with the population it served.

The Office of Cross-Google Engineering runs enablement and growth programs for roughly 1,000 Principal, Distinguished, and Google Fellow engineers — the top 0.5% of a 180,000-person organization. These are not engineers who tolerate friction quietly. They notice latency. They notice process debt. They will tell you, precisely and without softening, when an operational system is below the standard they expect from the organization that asked them to trust it. The mentorship matching program was producing good outcomes — ICs were being connected to the right mentors, cohort quality was real — but the back-end work to get there was 3.5 hours of administrative processing per match. At the volume xGE operates, that's not a staffing problem. It's an architecture problem.

The transition I architected moved the program from manual cohort matching to an AI-driven internal platform. That sentence is shorter than the work. The actual problem wasn't technical — it was structural: any AI-driven matching system is only as good as its intake data, and the intake data was inconsistent. Mentors and mentees filled out profiles with wildly different levels of specificity. Some entries were three sentences. Some were three paragraphs. The signal-to-noise ratio on the input side meant that automating the matching layer without cleaning the intake layer would have just made the wrong matches faster. So I worked backward — redesigned the intake structure first, tightened the prompt architecture around what the model actually needed to produce a defensible match recommendation, then built the matching layer on top of cleaner inputs. The administrative processing time dropped from 3.5 hours per match to under 20 minutes. That's a 90% reduction in processing time per match — and it compounds. The same architecture that handled current cohort volume was now capable of scaling active deployment capacity by more than 300% for H1 2026 without adding headcount.

The result matters beyond the efficiency number. At the senior IC tier, attrition is not a volume problem — it's a cost-per-departure problem. Each percentage point of senior IC attrition at that organizational tier carries estimated eight-figure cost implications. A mentorship program that functions as a retention lever, that matches the right mentor to the right Fellow or Distinguished Engineer with enough speed and precision that the IC actually trusts the process — that's not HR infrastructure. That's risk management at engineering scale. The 300% deployment capacity expansion means the program can absorb growth in the senior IC population without the back-end becoming the bottleneck again. The architecture was built to stay ahead of the load, not to catch up with it.

What I keep returning to is the intake problem — because it's the same problem I'd encountered in broadcast production, just wearing different clothing. At HuffPost Live, we were integrating audience comment streams into live segment flow with no edit-room buffer. The failure mode wasn't the live air window. It was the intake layer: if you didn't structure what came in, the segment fell apart in real time, on camera, with no second take. The discipline of cleaning the signal before it hits the production layer is the same instinct whether the production layer is a live broadcast or an LLM matching engine. The tools change. The structural logic doesn't. Enterprise DevRel at scale has the same intake problem — developer signals coming in at volume, inconsistent quality, and the temptation to automate before the inputs are clean. I've been solving that problem, in one form or another, for fifteen years.
