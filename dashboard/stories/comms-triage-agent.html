The problem wasn't volume. It was classification debt — every inbound communications request hitting the Office of Cross-Google Engineering landed in the same undifferentiated pile, and sorting it manually was eating hours that should have gone to the work itself. I was the Internal Communications Lead for a team serving roughly 1,000 of Google's most senior technical ICs — Principal, Distinguished, and Fellow engineers, the top 0.5% of a 180,000-person organization — and the triage layer between them and inbound noise simply didn't exist.

The architecture I designed has three prompts in sequence: triage, revise, escalate. That ordering is deliberate. The first prompt classifies inbound traffic into Low, Medium, or High touch tiers — not by keyword match, but by reading request complexity, stakeholder surface area, and downstream risk. The second prompt handles the Low-touch tier autonomously, applying Smart Brevity principles and generating a documented rationale for what it changed and why. The third prompt fires only when the first two identify something that needs a human — elevated stakeholder risk, ambiguous scope, anything carrying reputational weight at the VP level or above. The conditional KB loading is what makes the tier boundaries hold: the agent pulls different knowledge base context depending on classification, so a Low-touch response about program logistics and a High-touch escalation note for an engineering mandate decision are drawing on genuinely different bodies of institutional knowledge, not the same flat prompt with different labels.

One design choice I'm still satisfied with: I kept this as a single-agent system rather than a multi-agent orchestration. The temptation with a three-stage workflow is to spin up three agents and treat them as independent workers. That introduces coordination overhead, state handoff risk, and debugging surface area you don't need when the routing logic is clean. A single Gemini agent running three sequential prompts with conditional branching does the same work with less failure surface — and in a production environment serving principals at this seniority, failure surface is exactly what you're managing against. The system is not a demo. It cannot explain itself to a Fellow engineer with a deadline.

The system recaptured approximately 160 operational hours per year at greater than 90% classification accuracy across the full inbound stream. Those aren't projected figures — that's the measured output of a deployed agent. The more stringent test came during a multi-month medical leave in early 2026: the triage system maintained 100% operational continuity without a single human intervention. No escalation failures, no misrouted requests, no backlog accumulation. The program kept running at the same classification accuracy while the person who built it was offline for months. That's the standard a production agent has to meet — not performance on a demo track, but unattended reliability under conditions the builder didn't anticipate.

What I take from this isn't primarily technical. The prompt architecture was the easier part. The harder part was scoping the classification taxonomy precisely enough that "Low touch" and "High touch" meant the same thing across 1,000 users with different working styles and different tolerances for automated response. That required the same editorial judgment I was running in live broadcast — reading a signal fast, assigning it the right weight, and making a routing decision before the next one arrives. The newsroom decade wasn't preparation for this. It was the same discipline, applied to a different kind of inbound traffic.
