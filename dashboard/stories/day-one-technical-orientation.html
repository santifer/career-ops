Build content that scales

Source: Sierra — Developer Relations Engineer (SF) · Humanize: 4% unknown

The content I built at Google didn't start as a content strategy. It started as a provocation: 75,000 people were about to join the company, and the system designed to orient them technically on Day One wasn't built to scale. It was built to be filmed once and forgotten.

When I inherited the Day One technical orientation project as a Senior Communications and Content Manager in Corporate Engineering, the existing material was functional in the way that most institutional video is functional — it answered questions, it ran on time, and it hadn't been updated in long enough that nobody was entirely sure when it had been made. What it didn't do was treat its audience as an engineering population with specific cognitive habits, a high tolerance for information density, and zero patience for content that assumed they needed things explained slowly. It also didn't account for the fact that a new hire class at Google is not a stable noun. It's a continuous variable. The content couldn't scale by being redone annually. It had to scale by being built right.

The challenge wasn't creative. It was architectural. Content that scales isn't content that's popular — it's content whose production infrastructure can absorb volume without degrading. That meant rethinking the piece not as a video project but as a system with inputs, outputs, and a feedback mechanism. I rebuilt the orientation from the structural layer up: modular sequencing so individual sections could be updated independently without requiring a full re-shoot, a production logic that separated evergreen explanatory material from version-specific procedural steps, and a delivery architecture that got hardware provisioning instructions in front of new hires at the exact moment they needed them — not before, when it would be ignored, and not after, when the damage was already done. The editorial discipline here was the same one I'd applied at AJ+ when designing the day-of explainer format: identify the decision point the audience is actually facing, build the content backward from that moment, and cut everything that doesn't serve it. At AJ+, the decision point was the thirty seconds a viewer spent deciding whether to keep scrolling. At Google, it was the first hour a new hire spent alone with a piece of corporate hardware, trying to figure out whether they were doing it right.

The result was measurable in a way that editorial content rarely is: 88 percent of the 75,000-plus new hires who went through the rebuilt orientation provisioned their own corporate hardware autonomously within the first 24 hours. That number matters not just as a success metric but as a proof of format. A new hire provisioning their own hardware without escalating to IT support is a new hire who encountered instructions calibrated precisely to their actual moment of confusion — not a new hire who got lucky. Eighty-eight percent is a system working. It's also a number that compounds: at 75,000 new hires, the difference between 88 percent autonomous provisioning and, say, 70 percent is roughly 13,500 support escalations that either do or don't hit the IT queue. Content at scale isn't an abstract aspiration. It has a ticket count.

What I took from that project — and what I'd take into a developer relations context at a company like Sierra — is that the production discipline for content that scales is identical whether you're orienting engineers at a global technology company or building developer documentation for an AI platform. The question is always the same: what is the exact moment the person on the other end is stuck, and does this content reach them there? Everything else is production overhead. The format, the platform, the runtime — those are variables. The architectural logic underneath them isn't.
