# US Job Market Intelligence

Comprehensive context for evaluating, applying to, and negotiating roles in the United States market.

---

## Compensation Conventions

### Total Compensation (TC)

In the US tech market, compensation is expressed as **Total Compensation (TC)**, which typically includes:

- **Base Salary** — fixed annual cash compensation, paid biweekly or semi-monthly
- **Equity** — stock grants that vest over time, the largest variable component at senior levels
- **Bonus** — annual performance bonus, typically 10-25% of base at tech companies
- **Sign-On Bonus** — one-time cash payment, common when bridging a gap from unvested equity at a previous employer

### Equity Types

- **RSUs (Restricted Stock Units)** — most common at public companies. Vest on a schedule (typically 4 years with 1-year cliff). Taxed as ordinary income at vesting.
- **ISOs (Incentive Stock Options)** — tax-advantaged options available only to employees. Common at startups. Subject to AMT considerations.
- **NSOs (Non-Qualified Stock Options)** — options without ISO tax benefits. Can be granted to contractors and advisors. Taxed as ordinary income on exercise spread.

### Vesting Schedules

- Standard: 4-year vest with 1-year cliff (25% at month 12, then monthly or quarterly)
- Amazon notably back-loads: 5% / 15% / 40% / 40% over 4 years
- Some companies offer 3-year vesting or immediate vesting with lower grant sizes
- Refresher grants are common at large tech companies (annual additional RSU grants)

### Research Sources

- **Levels.fyi** — crowdsourced TC data with breakdowns by level, company, and location
- **Glassdoor** — salary ranges and interview data
- **Blind** — anonymous professional network with compensation threads
- **Payscale / Salary.com** — broader market data beyond tech
- **H1B Salary Database** — public record of salaries for visa-sponsored positions

### Location Bands

Many US companies use location-based pay bands:
- **Tier 1** — San Francisco, New York City, Seattle (+0% to reference)
- **Tier 2** — Los Angeles, Boston, Washington DC, Austin (-5% to -15%)
- **Tier 3** — Denver, Chicago, Atlanta, Portland (-10% to -25%)
- **Tier 4** — Fully remote / low-cost-of-living areas (-15% to -30%)

Some companies (GitLab, Basecamp, Buffer) pay the same regardless of location. This is increasingly rare.

## Legal & Cultural Context

### At-Will Employment

The US follows **at-will employment** in 49 of 50 states (Montana is the exception). Either party can terminate the relationship at any time, for any reason (or no reason), as long as it is not discriminatory.

- No mandatory notice period (though 2 weeks is customary)
- No statutory severance requirement
- Many companies offer severance packages (1-4 weeks per year of service) as goodwill or in exchange for a release of claims

### Non-Compete Agreements

- The FTC proposed a nationwide ban on non-competes in 2024; enforcement remains in flux
- California, Minnesota, North Dakota, and Oklahoma already ban most non-competes
- Where enforceable, non-competes typically last 6-24 months and are limited by geography and scope
- Non-solicitation agreements (cannot recruit former colleagues/clients) are more commonly enforced

### Background Checks

- Standard for most corporate roles; typically run after offer acceptance
- Criminal history, education verification, employment verification, credit check (finance roles)
- Some states/cities have "ban the box" laws preventing criminal history questions on applications

### Work Authorization & Visas

- H-1B: specialty occupation visa, annual cap with lottery (April filing, October start)
- L-1: intracompany transfer visa
- O-1: extraordinary ability visa (no cap)
- TN: USMCA visa for Canadian and Mexican citizens
- OPT/STEM OPT: post-graduation work authorization for F-1 students (12 months + 24-month STEM extension)
- Green Card sponsorship: employer-sponsored EB-2/EB-3 process, can take 1-10+ years depending on country of birth

### Benefits

- **Health Insurance** — employer-sponsored is standard; quality varies significantly. Ask about deductible, out-of-pocket max, and network.
- **401(k)** — retirement savings with employer match (typically 3-6% of salary). Some companies offer mega backdoor Roth.
- **PTO** — no federal mandate. Tech companies typically offer 15-25 days + holidays. "Unlimited PTO" policies average 10-15 days actually taken.
- **Parental Leave** — no federal paid leave. Tech companies typically offer 12-20 weeks paid; some offer equal paternity leave.
- **COBRA** — continuation of health coverage after leaving a job (18 months, but employee pays full premium).

### Equity Cliff

The 1-year cliff is standard. If you leave before 12 months, you typically forfeit all unvested equity. For ISOs, you usually have 90 days post-departure to exercise vested options (some companies extend to 10 years).

## Outreach Norms

- **LinkedIn DMs** — standard practice for professional networking and recruiting. Both cold outreach from candidates and recruiter inbound are normal.
- **Direct Email** — acceptable, especially to hiring managers. Finding work emails via Hunter.io, Apollo, or company patterns is common.
- **15-Minute Coffee Chat** — a normal ask. Virtual or in-person. Frame as "learning about the team" rather than "asking for a job."
- **Follow-Up** — one follow-up after 7 days is expected and professional. Two follow-ups maximum. After that, move on.

## Interview Process

A typical US tech interview process follows these steps:

1. **Application** — resume submission via job board, referral, or direct application
2. **Recruiter Screen** — 15-30 min phone/video call covering background, salary expectations, timeline, visa status
3. **Hiring Manager Screen** — 30-45 min call focused on role fit, team dynamics, and high-level technical discussion
4. **Technical Assessment** — take-home project (2-6 hours) OR live coding screen (45-60 min on CoderPad/HackerRank)
5. **On-Site / Virtual Loop** — 3-5 interviews in a single day covering technical depth, system design, behavioral (STAR format), and culture fit
6. **Reference Check** — 2-3 professional references contacted after final round
7. **Offer** — verbal offer followed by written offer letter. Typical response deadline: 1-2 weeks (negotiable).

### Common Interview Formats

- **Behavioral** — STAR method (Situation, Task, Action, Result). Prepare 8-12 stories covering leadership, conflict, failure, impact.
- **System Design** — whiteboard or virtual diagramming. Focus on trade-offs, scalability, and communication.
- **Coding** — LeetCode-style (FAANG) or practical problems. Python/JavaScript most common languages accepted.
- **Case Study** — increasingly common for product and applied roles. Present analysis and recommendations.

## Salary Negotiation

### Key Principles

- **Always negotiate.** 70%+ of hiring managers expect it. Failing to negotiate can leave 10-20% TC on the table.
- **Negotiate TC, not just base.** Equity, sign-on bonus, and start date are often more flexible than base salary.
- **Use competing offers.** The strongest lever. Even a verbal competing offer changes the dynamic.
- **Delay giving a number first.** If pressed: "I'm targeting competitive compensation for this level in [location]."
- **Get it in writing.** Verbal promises about equity refresh, promotion timeline, or remote work mean nothing without documentation.

### Timing

- Negotiate after the written offer, not before
- Ask for 2-3 business days to review (standard and expected)
- Make a single, clear counter-proposal rather than multiple rounds
- If they cannot budge on comp, negotiate start date, sign-on, title, or remote flexibility
