# US Outreach Conventions

Guidelines for professional outreach in the US job market. These norms apply to hiring managers, recruiters, and referral contacts.

---

## LinkedIn Direct Message

LinkedIn DMs are the default outreach channel in the US market. Connection requests with a note have higher acceptance rates than InMail.

### Constraints
- **300 character maximum** for connection request notes
- InMail has a higher character limit but lower response rates
- Messages should be mobile-readable (most LinkedIn activity is on mobile)

### Structure
1. **Specific reference** — mention something concrete about their work, team, or a recent company announcement. Generic flattery is ignored.
2. **One proof point** — a single relevant achievement or credential that establishes credibility. Do not list multiple.
3. **Clear ask** — state exactly what you want (a 15-minute chat, a referral, feedback on your application). Vague messages get no response.

### Example (Connection Request)
> Hi [Name] — saw your team shipped [specific project]. I led a similar effort at [Company] that [one-line result]. Would love 15 min to learn about [specific thing]. Open to a quick chat this week?

## Email

Direct email to hiring managers is common and acceptable in the US market. Finding professional emails through Hunter.io, Apollo.io, or pattern guessing (firstname@company.com) is standard practice.

### Structure
- **3-5 sentences maximum.** Anything longer will not be read.
- **Specific subject line** — include the role name or a mutual connection. Never use generic subjects like "Opportunity" or "Quick Question."
- **No fluff.** Skip "I hope this email finds you well." Open with the reason you are writing.

### Example
> Subject: [Role Title] — [Your relevant credential in 5 words]
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm a [title] with [X years] in [relevant domain]. Your team's work on [specific project] caught my attention because [connection to your experience].
>
> I applied through [channel] and wanted to share one thing that might not come through on paper: [one compelling proof point with a number].
>
> Would you have 15 minutes this week for a quick call? Happy to work around your schedule.

## Follow-Up Cadence

| Day | Action |
|-----|--------|
| Day 0 | Send initial outreach (LinkedIn DM or email) |
| Day 7 | Send one follow-up if no response. Reference the original message. Add one new piece of value (an article, a relevant insight, a portfolio link). |
| Day 14+ | **Stop.** No further follow-ups. If they have not responded after two touches, they are not interested or not the right contact. Move on to alternative contacts. |

### Follow-Up Guidelines
- Never apologize for following up ("Sorry to bother you again"). It signals low status.
- Each follow-up should add value, not just ask again.
- If they respond but are non-committal ("Let me get back to you"), wait 10 days before one final check-in.

## Referral Request

When asking someone in your network to refer you internally:

### Structure
1. **Make it easy.** Provide a link to the specific job posting and a 2-sentence blurb they can copy-paste into the referral form.
2. **Explain the fit.** Give them one concrete reason why you are a strong candidate so they can vouch with confidence.
3. **Respect the relationship.** Only ask people who have seen your work or know you professionally. Cold referral requests from strangers are ignored.

### Example
> Hi [Name] — I saw [Company] has an opening for [Role Title] ([link]). Given my work on [specific project/skill], I think it could be a strong fit. Would you be open to submitting a referral? Here's a quick blurb you can use:
>
> "[Your Name] is a [title] with [X years] in [domain]. They led [one-line achievement]. I've worked with them on [context] and can speak to their [specific strength]."
>
> Totally understand if the timing doesn't work — appreciate you either way.
