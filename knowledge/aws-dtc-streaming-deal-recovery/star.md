# STAR Framework: AWS DTC Streaming Deal Recovery

**Interview Context:** External audience (VP Sales, Chief Customer Officer, Engagement Leadership, Founder); demonstrates crisis management, executive presence, customer empathy, business acumen

---

## SITUATION

AWS had a customer — a DTC streaming platform company with an ambitious vision. The CEO wanted to build a white-label streaming platform that other organizations could license and deploy: universities could create their own video library and sell access to students, nonprofits could monetize educational content, corporations could build internal video platforms.

It was a good vision. It was a big contract. AWS was brought in to architect and build it.

But the engagement failed. The previous AWS team completely misunderstood the requirements. They built a monolithic, complex system that didn't match the customer's vision at all. The CEO was devastated — significant time, significant budget, and the deliverable didn't solve his problem.

**The Situation:** CEO was ready to leave AWS and go to a competitor. This was a named customer about to become a reference for why you should NOT use AWS. Beyond the immediate contract value at risk, this was a reputational issue in the streaming/media market.

I was brought in to save it.

---

## TASK

My job was straightforward but high-stakes: understand why the engagement failed, rebuild the customer's trust in AWS, get alignment on what the customer actually needed, and negotiate a path forward that would result in successful delivery.

This was not a technical architecture role. I wasn't going to design the streaming platform — I was brought in for the strategic and interpersonal layer. The question was: can you fix this relationship and set up a new delivery team for success?

---

## ACTION

**Executive Listening:**
I met with the CEO and actually listened. Not defensively, not with sales spin, but genuine curiosity: What were you trying to build? What did AWS deliver? Where is the gap? What would success look like?

The CEO was frustrated but willing to talk. As I listened, the problem became clear: the previous team had built a complex monolithic system when what the customer needed was a multi-tenant, deployable platform. Different architectural approach entirely.

**Honest Assessment:**
I went back to AWS and did an honest post-mortem. What happened? Why was the previous team so far off base? The answer: they didn't ask the right questions upfront. They made assumptions about what "streaming platform" meant and built something that technically worked, but wasn't what the customer needed.

**Problem Articulation:**
The key insight was communicating this clearly to the CEO: "Here's what went wrong. Here's why the team made those choices. Here's what we're going to do differently. Here's how we're going to make sure this doesn't happen again."

That honesty mattered. The CEO had been prepared for AWS to defend the previous work or minimize the problem. Instead, I acknowledged failure clearly.

**Requirements Redefinition:**
I facilitated a deep-dive conversation about what the customer actually needed. What does "white-label deployable" mean in practice? How will customers deploy instances? How much customization should be possible? What does the tech stack need to support?

I documented everything. I got alignment. I had the customer validate that I understood correctly. These requirements became the foundation for the new engagement.

**Commercial Renegotiation:**
This is the hard part. The customer had paid for work that didn't meet expectations. AWS had to acknowledge that and offer some form of remedy. I negotiated:
- A credit or refund for the failed work
- A new contract for the properly-scoped engagement
- Clear success criteria and delivery timelines
- Pricing that reflected the actual scope

The result: $375K new contract that properly reflected what needed to be built. A renegotiation that was fair to both sides.

**Transition Setup:**
I brought in a new delivery team with a fresh perspective. But I didn't just hand off — I made sure they understood the customer's vision, the failed previous attempt, and the critical requirements. I introduced them to the CEO and established the relationship.

Then I rolled off. Not because the problem was solved (it wasn't — the platform still needed to be built), but because the relationship was restored and the next team was set up for success with clear context.

**Honest Self-Positioning:**
I was transparent with the customer about my role. "I'm here to understand what went wrong, help us get alignment on what you actually need, and make sure the next team succeeds. I'm not a streaming platform architect — I'm a strategic account manager. Once we've got the requirements clear and a new team ramped up, I'll step back and let them execute."

That honesty was important. The customer appreciated that I wasn't overselling or pretending to have expertise I didn't have. And the new team respected that I was setting them up with clear requirements and customer alignment rather than ambiguity.

---

## RESULT

**Immediate Recovery:**
- Reversed CEO's decision to leave AWS for competitor
- Rebuilt trust through honest assessment and transparent problem-solving
- Secured $375K new contract with properly-scoped engagement
- Established clear requirements and success criteria for new team

**Commercial Outcome:**
- $9M in AWS platform revenue over 24 months (from the successfully built platform)
- Revenue growing beyond the 24-month window (platform adoption expanding)
- Customer transformed from "we're leaving" to "AWS is our partner"
- Positive reference and case study in streaming/media market

**Operational Success:**
- New delivery team successfully built the multi-tenant streaming platform
- Customer achieved vision: white-label platform deployable by other organizations
- Platform functionality: tenant creation, content management, monetization, analytics
- Customer satisfaction restored

**Lessons & Learning:**
- The failure wasn't about technology — it was about requirements gathering
- Honesty and empathy matter more than defensive posturing when things go wrong
- Setting up a new team with clear context is as important as the team's technical capability
- Sometimes your value is in facilitation and relationship recovery, not technical depth
