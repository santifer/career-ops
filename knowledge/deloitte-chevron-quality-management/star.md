# Chevron Quality Management — STAR Framework (Interview)

## THE CHALLENGE (Situation)

I joined Deloitte's massive SAP implementation at Chevron (Fortune 10 energy company) as an analyst. The company was deploying a new enterprise platform across all US downstream refineries—their largest operations segment with ~8 facilities including the nation's largest refinery.

The core problem: Chevron's quality testing process was fragmented, slow, and unreliable. Materials arrived via train, barge, and rail—had to be inspected against complex tolerance specs. If they didn't meet standards, they could contaminate everything downstream. The manual inspection process was a massive bottleneck. Finished product quality suffered, production delays mounted, and the company was burning money.

Beyond the operational problem, there was a critical adoption challenge: the end users were blue collar refinery workers on 24/7 shift cycles. They weren't tech-savvy, they didn't want software that slowed them down, and they were skeptical of change. Deploying a complex quality system to them and actually getting them to USE it? That was a whole different beast.

---

## THE ACTION (Task)

I started as an analyst doing grunt work on the project. But I saw an opportunity: instead of just following playbooks, I hacked together customizations to the cutover tools that Deloitte was using. I tailored them specifically for Chevron's needs—automation they didn't know they could get. The leadership team noticed. They pulled me off grunt work and onto the product management team.

Once on the product side, I owned an ambitious mandate: **build four end-to-end quality management applications that would handle the entire inspection lifecycle**—from raw material acceptance through finished product validation—and actually get refinery workers to adopt them.

Here's what I did:

### 1. Deep User Discovery (4 AM Shifts)
I didn't do user research from a conference room. I embedded myself with shift workers at 4 AM, watching how they actually worked, what frustrated them, what they'd never use. I sat through entire 24-hour shift cycles across multiple refineries. I learned that one-size-fits-all software doesn't work on the production floor. Every shift had different constraints. Every team had different priorities.

### 2. Product Development (SAP BTP)
I architected and built four interconnected products on SAP Business Technology Platform:
- **Raw Material Inspection:** Automated quality gate for incoming materials
- **Tolerance Validation:** Real-time specs matching against vendor requirements
- **Finished Product Testing:** Post-production quality validation
- **Quality Holds & Escalation:** Intelligent workflow routing for exceptions

Each product was modular enough to deploy independently, but they worked together as a system. Every feature was born from what I learned in those 4 AM shifts.

### 3. Adoption Engine (Feedback-Driven Iteration)
I built a real-time feedback loop. Every product had embedded KPI tracking—usage patterns, behavioral data, where people were struggling. I'd review the data, iterate the product, and push updates. Then I'd go back to the refineries and watch what changed. Did adoption go up? Did efficiency improve? If not, I'd dig into why and iterate again.

This wasn't a one-time training-and-done model. This was continuous iteration with real operational data driving every decision.

### 4. Scaling Across 8 Refineries
I managed deployment across all 8 US downstream refineries, each with different configurations, shift structures, and operational pressures. I had to scale the adoption approach—training shift supervisors as change champions, building shift-specific documentation, managing dependencies between refineries.

## THE RESULT (Result)

**The metrics speak for themselves:**

**Revenue Impact:** $56M quarterly revenue impact across the four products. That's $224M annually—this wasn't a cost reduction project, this was a revenue-generation product line.

**Operational Efficiency:** 31% improvement in production operations. That's not a 3% tweak; that's moving the needle at a refinery-scale level.

**User Adoption:** 4x adoption growth in 12 months. Going from skeptical blue collar workers to people who couldn't imagine doing their jobs without these tools—that's a story.

**Quality Improvements:** Measurable increases in first-time blend rates, reduced quality holds, faster inspection cycles. The products actually solved the problem they were designed to solve.

**Scale:** All 8 US downstream refineries, 24/7 shift coverage, thousands of end users across multiple facilities.

**Technical Achievement:** Proved that SAP Business Technology Platform could handle Fortune 10-scale custom quality applications. This became a reference architecture for future extensions.

---

## WHY THIS MATTERS

This project taught me three things that I've carried forward:

**1. User-Centric Product Development Isn't Nice—It's Essential**
I could have built a technically perfect system that nobody used. Instead, I spent time understanding WHO was using it and WHY. That insight transformed adoption from a blocker into a competitive advantage.

**2. Feedback Loops Are More Valuable Than Perfect Initial Design**
The products that worked best weren't the ones I designed perfectly in the first version. They were the ones where I built the feedback mechanism into the product itself and iterated relentlessly. Usage data beats assumptions every time.

**3. Enterprise Adoption Happens at the Margins**
The hardest part wasn't building the software. It was getting a production worker on the night shift at a refinery to actually use it instead of working around it. That's where the real work is, and that's where most enterprise initiatives fail.

## POTENTIAL INTERVIEW QUESTIONS & BRIDGES

**Q: Tell me about a time you had to make a difficult tradeoff.**
Bridge: The quality management project forced constant tradeoffs between feature completeness and adoption speed. Early on, I wanted to build the perfect system. But feedback from the field showed that simpler, faster tools were getting 10x more usage. I learned to optimize for adoption over perfection.

**Q: How do you measure success?**
Bridge: For this project, I could have used traditional software metrics—lines of code, uptime, bugs fixed. But the actual success metrics were operational: adoption rate, efficiency gains, revenue impact, and user feedback scores. I built the product roadmap around those metrics, not technical metrics.

**Q: Tell me about a time you influenced a large organization.**
Bridge: Shifting 8 refineries and thousands of shift workers from a manual process to a digital system required influencing at multiple levels—from shift supervisors (change champions) to area leads to executive sponsors. I did that by making the case with data, not politics.

**Q: How do you handle failure or setbacks?**
Bridge: The biggest early setback was low initial adoption. I had built a great product, but workers weren't using it. Instead of assuming they were wrong, I went back to the field, watched what was actually happening, and realized I'd missed critical workflow steps. That feedback loop—and willingness to rethink—is what ultimately drove adoption.
