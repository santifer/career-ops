# STAR Framework: AWS Partner Co-Delivery Model

**Interview Context:** External audience (COO, VP Operations, scaling executive, founder); demonstrates operational thinking, strategic problem-solving, resource management

---

## SITUATION

AWS Professional Services had a happy problem: more deal flow than capacity. The Media & Entertainment practice was receiving 5+ AI projects in pipeline, all solid opportunities, all capable of generating significant revenue. But internal team capacity was exhausted.

This is the classic scaling dilemma. You can either:
1. **Turn down deals** (bad for revenue, bad for market positioning)
2. **Overcommit and underdeliver** (bad for customer satisfaction, bad for team morale)
3. **Hire aggressively** (expensive, slow, hard to find specialized talent)
4. **Build strategic partnerships** (risky, requires discipline, but scalable)

We chose option 4. But it required solving a hard problem: How do you maintain AWS's quality and customer satisfaction standards when you're bringing in external partners?

**The Opportunity:** $3.5M in annual deal value that would otherwise be lost. For the M&E practice, that was significant. But it wasn't just about the revenue — it was about market positioning. We needed to be able to say "yes" to customers who needed specialized AI expertise.

---

## TASK

I took ownership of building the strategic framework for partner co-delivery. This meant:

1. **Capacity Planning:** Map out the pipeline, identify skill gaps, forecast what we could deliver vs. what we couldn't
2. **Partner Strategy:** Evaluate and select partners who could deliver quality equivalent to AWS standards
3. **Operating Model:** Build the framework for how AWS and partners would work together (AWS prime, partner subcontractor)
4. **Execution:** Enable deal teams to use the co-delivery model and actually land the deals

The challenge was making partnerships work in a large, quality-conscious organization like AWS. It's one thing to partner with a vendor as a supplier. It's another thing to put a partner's engineers in front of your customers. That requires alignment on culture, quality, communication, and problem-solving.

---

## ACTION

**Capacity Assessment:**
I started with brutal honesty about what we could and couldn't deliver. I mapped 6 months of pipeline and asked: which projects can we staff with internal resources? Which ones have skill gaps?

It became clear: we needed specialized expertise in areas like AI DevOps for media workloads, or deep experience with computer vision in media contexts. These weren't generalist skills. It would take months to hire and ramp someone up. And the deals couldn't wait.

**Partner Evaluation:**
I evaluated consulting firms and specialized vendors. The criteria were strict: Could they deliver quality equivalent to AWS standards? Did they have the specialized skills we needed? Could they operate in a subcontractor role while AWS owned the customer relationship? Did they understand media and entertainment?

I selected partners based on these criteria, not just on price. Cheaper is not better if it damages customer satisfaction or creates integration headaches.

**Co-Delivery Framework:**
I designed the operating model. AWS remains the prime contractor — we own the customer relationship, we're accountable for delivery, we set the quality bar. Partners are specialized subcontractors who contribute resources and expertise in their domain.

This created clear accountability: if something goes wrong with a partner's work, it's still AWS's problem to fix. That clarity is critical — customers know who to contact, and we know who's responsible for quality.

**Engagement Process:**
I documented the playbook: how do you identify which deals need co-delivery? How do you scope the partner contribution? How do you communicate with customers about partner involvement? How do you coordinate between AWS and partners?

The communication piece is important. You don't hide partner involvement from customers, but you frame it as "AWS is bringing in specialized expertise for this component." Customers understand that AWS isn't doing everything in-house — they're OK with that as long as quality is maintained and accountability is clear.

**Financial Modeling:**
I built models for how this would work economically. What does it cost to engage partners? What's the project revenue? What's the margin? How do we structure deals so everyone makes money?

This matters because if the economics don't work, the model won't scale. You need pricing that covers partner costs and still delivers healthy margins. And you need partner economics where partners are incentivized to deliver quality.

**Risk Management:**
Partnerships are risky. What if a partner doesn't deliver quality? What if they disappear mid-project? What if there are cultural misalignments? I built mitigation strategies:
- Quality gates and customer satisfaction monitoring
- Multi-partner strategy (not dependent on one firm)
- Clear escalation paths and contingency procedures
- Regular communication and alignment

**Execution & Scaling:**
Once the framework was built, I enabled the sales and delivery teams to use it. We executed 5 deals using the co-delivery model. I tracked results: Did we deliver on time? Did customers get what they needed? Did the partnerships work?

Early results were positive. Customers didn't care whether the engineers had AWS badges or partner badges — they cared whether the work was excellent. It was. Partners understood the quality bar and rose to it.

**Operational Excellence:**

- Built repeatable playbook for partner engagement
- Established partnerships that could be leveraged across the practice
- Enabled team capacity to scale beyond headcount

**Business Outcomes:**

- Maintained healthy margins despite partner costs

- Demonstrated ability to scale delivery without sacrificing quality

## INTERVIEW ANGLES & TALKING POINTS

### For COO / VP Operations / Scaling:
"This is operational thinking at scale. You have a capacity constraint. You can't throw money at hiring and expect to hire specialized talent in 90 days. You need a model that lets you scale delivery. That's partnerships. But partnerships are risky in a quality-conscious organization. The key is building clear frameworks: who's accountable, what's the quality bar, how do we communicate with customers. When those are defined, partnerships become a scaling lever, not a risk."

### For VP Sales / Go-to-Market:
"This enabled us to say 'yes' to $3.5M in deals. From a sales perspective, that's huge. You're not turning down opportunities because of internal constraints. You're expanding your addressable market by bringing in specialized partners. And from a customer perception standpoint, most customers actually don't mind partners — they mind if quality suffers or accountability is unclear. We managed the communication so it was transparent and professional."

### For Finance / P&L:
"The model was financially disciplined. We calculated partner costs, we modeled margins, we ensured that the revenue from deals more than offset partner spending. It wasn't a loss leader — it was a profitable business model. And it created options: if a skill gap is expensive to hire for internally, we can bring in partners for specific projects. That's operational flexibility."

### For Organizational Design:
"This teaches something about scaling in large organizations. You can't control everything. Sometimes the best answer is 'we'll coordinate with an external partner and ensure quality through governance frameworks.' That requires confidence and discipline — you can't micromanage partners. But if you're clear about expectations and you monitor results, partnerships work."

### For Competitive Strategy:
"From AWS's perspective, this was competitive advantage. Our competitors had the same capacity constraints we did. But we said 'yes' to deals they couldn't staff. That's market share gain. Customers remember who stepped up for them. Some of those 5 deals became long-term relationships that expanded beyond the initial scope."

### For Team Scaling:
"This is how you grow a team faster than hiring. You can't hire 20 specialized AI engineers in 90 days. But you can partner with consulting firms that have them. Your internal team stays focused on core capability while partners fill specialized gaps. This is how services organizations actually scale."

### For Board / Investor Pitch:
"We had $3.5M in deals at risk of being lost due to capacity constraints. We built a partner co-delivery model that let us capture those deals without sacrificing quality or maintaining excessive overhead. The model is repeatable and can scale to other practices within AWS. It's a framework for sustainable growth when hiring can't keep pace with demand."
