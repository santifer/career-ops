# Project Details: AWS Partner Co-Delivery Model for Media & Entertainment

**Timeline:** 2022-2023 (parallel to M&E customer work)
**Organization:** Amazon Web Services (Professional Services, M&E Practice)
**Scope:** Partner strategy and capacity planning for AI engagements
**Impact:** Unlocked $3.5M in pipeline that would have been lost

---

## Context & Problem Statement

AWS Professional Services faced a classic capacity bottleneck in the Media & Entertainment practice. The AI opportunity pipeline was growing faster than internal team capacity. The practice was receiving more project inquiries than it could staff with AWS resources alone.

**The Business Dilemma:**
- **Opportunity Pipeline:** 5+ AI/ML projects in pipeline that AWS couldn't execute
- **Skill Gaps:** Projects required specialized skills (AI DevOps, media asset tagging, ML infrastructure) that AWS didn't have in-house
- **Capacity Constraints:** Internal engineering team was fully allocated to existing customer work
- **Market Opportunity Lost:** Without a solution, AWS would have to decline deals or suggest competitors' services
- **Deal Value at Risk:** Estimated $3.5M in annual contract value would be lost

The question was: How do we say "yes" to deals we don't have internal capacity to deliver?

---

## Solution: Strategic Partner Co-Delivery Model

Jon designed and implemented a partner strategy that enabled AWS to pursue deals it couldn't staff alone by building strategic partnerships with specialized consulting firms.

### Phase 1: Capacity Assessment & Gap Analysis

**Engagement Mapping:**
- Cataloged all AI/ML engagements in pipeline over 6-month forward horizon
- Documented skill requirements for each project (AI engineering, ML DevOps, media asset processing, etc.)

**Internal Capacity Assessment:**
- Evaluated current team capacity and resource allocation
- Forecasted available capacity for next 6 months
- Identified which projects could be fully staffed with internal resources
- Identified which projects had unfillable skill gaps

**Skill Gap Identification:**
- Documented specialized skills needed but not available internally
- Examples: "AI DevOps engineer with media asset tagging experience," "ML infrastructure specialist," "Video processing expert"
- Prioritized skills by project demand and market availability

---

### Phase 2: Partner Ecosystem Evaluation

**Partner Assessment:**
- Evaluated external consulting firms and specialist vendors
- Assessed partner capabilities across identified skill gaps
- Evaluated financial models and billing structures
- Assessed cultural fit and quality standards
- Evaluated partner references and past delivery quality

**Strategic Selection:**
- Identified partners specialized in required skill areas
- Selected firms that could deliver quality equivalent to AWS standards
- Negotiated terms for co-delivery engagements (AWS prime, partner subcontract)
- Built contracts and operational frameworks

**Relationship Building:**
- Established ongoing partnership agreements
- Created clear scope delineation (AWS responsibilities vs. partner responsibilities)
- Built communication and coordination mechanisms
- Defined escalation paths and quality controls

---

### Phase 3: Capacity Planning & Workforce Strategy

**Forecasting Model:**
- Created 6-month rolling forecast of project pipeline by skill requirement
- Modeled internal resource availability by skill
- Calculated partner staffing needs to close skill gaps
- Projected capacity utilization and revenue impact

**Co-Delivery Framework:**
- Defined operating model: AWS as prime contractor, partner firms as specialized subcontractors
- Established clear responsibility boundaries
- Created handoff protocols for smooth collaboration
- Built quality assurance mechanisms

**Engagement Structure:**
- AWS owns customer relationship and overall solution architecture
- Partners contribute specialized resources and domain expertise
- AWS maintains accountability for delivery quality and customer satisfaction
- Partners are paid based on resource contribution (T&M or fixed scope models)

**Risk Management:**
- Identified risks in partner dependency
- Built mitigation strategies (multi-partner strategy, knowledge transfer)
- Established quality gates and customer satisfaction monitoring
- Created escalation and contingency procedures

---

### Phase 4: Implementation & Execution

**Deal Enablement:**
- First 5 deals utilized partner co-delivery model
- Trained deal teams on partner engagement process
- Established go-to-market messaging (positioned as "AWS + specialized expertise")
- Created sales enablement materials highlighting co-delivery advantage

**Operational Excellence:**
- Executed 5 engagements using co-delivery model
- Maintained customer satisfaction and quality standards
- Refined processes based on lessons learned
- Optimized partner coordination mechanisms

**Scaling Preparation:**
- Documented processes and playbooks for ongoing use
- Built institutional knowledge of partner capabilities
- Created repeatable model for future partner engagement
- Established metrics for partner performance tracking

## Technical & Operational Components

**Engagement Models:**
- **Team Augmentation:** Partner staff embedded in AWS team structures
- **Specialized Workstreams:** Partners own specific technical domains (e.g., AI DevOps infrastructure)
- **Prime Contractor Model:** AWS maintains customer relationship and overall delivery responsibility
- **Hybrid Governance:** Joint steering committees with partner leadership

**Quality Assurance:**
- AWS quality standards applied to all partner delivery
- Code reviews and architectural oversight by AWS
- Customer satisfaction monitoring across partners
- Escalation paths for quality issues

**Financial Models:**
- Time & Materials: Partner bills hourly, AWS marks up
- Fixed Scope: Partner quotes fixed price for deliverables
- Revenue Sharing: AWS and partner negotiate terms
- Incentive Alignment: Partner success tied to customer satisfaction

**Operational Protocols:**
- Partner onboarding and training on AWS standards
- Weekly coordination meetings between AWS and partners
- Escalation procedures for issues or blockers
- Customer communication protocols (AWS is primary interface)
